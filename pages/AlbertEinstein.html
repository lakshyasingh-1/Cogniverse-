01   Biography

A Life That
Changed Everything

Albert Einstein was born in Ulm, Germany. After graduating from ETH Zürich, he worked as a patent clerk in Bern and published four landmark papers in 1905 — his 'Annus Mirabilis' — transforming physics forever.

His special relativity (1905) and general relativity (1915) remain the most accurate descriptions of space, time, and gravity ever devised. He won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 for explaining the photoelectric effect — ironically one of his more modest contributions.

After fleeing Nazi Germany in 1933, he settled at Princeton's Institute for Advanced Study, where he spent the rest of his life pursuing a unified field theory — a grand dream that eluded even him.

02   Equations

The Mathematics
of the Universe

E = mc²
Mass-energy equivalence: energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. Mass can be converted to enormous energy.
Gμν + Λgμν = 8πG/c⁴ · Tμν
Einstein Field Equations: describes how mass-energy curves spacetime, producing the effect we call gravity.
τ = t√(1 − v²/c²)
Time dilation: a moving clock runs slower relative to a stationary observer. Speed warps time itself.
E = hf
Photoelectric effect: energy of a photon equals Planck's constant times its frequency. Light is quantized.
λ = h / p
de Broglie wavelength (using Einstein's insight): all matter has wave-like properties, blurring the line between particle and wave.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Einstein Stumbled

⚠ General relativity is incompatible with quantum mechanics — the great unresolved tension at the heart of modern physics.
⚠ Rejected quantum indeterminacy with "God does not play dice" — but later experiments vindicated quantum mechanics completely.
⚠ Never completed his unified field theory despite three decades of relentless effort after 1925.
⚠ His cosmological constant Λ was initially seen as his "greatest blunder" — yet was later vindicated as dark energy.
⚠ The EPR paradox — his argument against quantum completeness — was decisively disproved by Bell's theorem experiments.
