Aristotle

01   Biography

A Life That
Named the World

Aristotle was born in Stagira, in the Chalcidian peninsula of Macedonia, the son of Nicomachus, court physician to King Amyntas II of Macedon. At seventeen he was sent to Athens to study at Plato's Academy, where he remained for twenty years — first as a student, then as a teacher — until Plato's death in 347 BCE. He was Plato's most brilliant pupil, and his most independent: where Plato sought truth in eternal, transcendent Forms, Aristotle sought it by looking carefully at the world in front of him.

After leaving Athens he spent years on the island of Lesbos studying marine biology with extraordinary precision — dissecting, classifying, and describing hundreds of species with an accuracy that astonished 19th-century naturalists when they rediscovered his zoological works. In 343 BCE he was summoned by Philip II of Macedon to tutor the thirteen-year-old Alexander, later the Great — one of history's most consequential teacher-student relationships.

Returning to Athens in 335 BCE, Aristotle founded his own school, the Lyceum. He taught while walking — giving rise to the epithet Peripatetic (from the Greek for "walking around"). In the twelve years of the Lyceum, he produced the extraordinary corpus that would define European philosophy, science, and logic for almost two millennia: treatises on logic, physics, metaphysics, biology, psychology, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics. When Alexander died in 323 BCE, Athens' anti-Macedonian sentiment turned against Aristotle, who fled to Chalcis, famously saying he would not give Athens a second chance to sin against philosophy — a reference to Socrates' execution. He died there the following year, aged 62.
  Major Achievements
  • Invented formal logic — the syllogism as a complete system of deductive reasoning
  • Founded the Lyceum, Athens — the second great school of ancient philosophy
  • First systematic zoology — classified over 500 animal species
  • Developed the four causes — a framework for scientific explanation
  • Ethics of eudaimonia — the flourishing life as the telos of human existence
  • Theory of the mean — virtue as the middle ground between excess and deficiency
  • Poetics — analysis of tragedy and catharsis; founding work of literary theory
  • Politics — classification of constitutions; founder of political science
  • Tutor of Alexander the Great (343–335 BCE)
02   Principles & Frameworks

The Architecture
of Knowledge

All A are B; All B are C; ∴ All A are C
The syllogism — Aristotle's invention of formal deductive logic. A valid argument whose conclusion follows necessarily from its premises regardless of content. Codified in the Prior Analytics, it remained the dominant logical system for over two thousand years, until Frege's predicate logic in 1879.
Material · Formal · Efficient · Final
The Four Causes — Aristotle's framework for complete scientific explanation: the material cause (what it is made of), the formal cause (its structure or pattern), the efficient cause (what brought it about), and the final cause (its purpose or end). No explanation is complete without all four.
Virtue = Mean(Excess, Deficiency)
The Doctrine of the Mean — each virtue is the intermediate between two vices: courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness; generosity between miserliness and profligacy. The mean is not arithmetic but relative to the person and situation — requiring practical wisdom (phronesis) to discern.
Eudaimonia = Ἐνέργεια κατ᾽ ἀρετήν
Eudaimonia — the highest human good — is "activity of the soul in accordance with virtue." Not a feeling or a state but an activity: the full exercise of distinctively human capacities over a complete life. The foundation of virtue ethics, revived in contemporary moral philosophy through Anscombe, MacIntyre, and Foot.
Form + Matter = Substance   (Hylomorphism)
Hylomorphism — every concrete substance is a compound of matter (hylē) and form (morphē). The form is what makes a thing the kind of thing it is; matter is what it is made of. A bronze sphere: the bronze is the matter, the spherical shape the form. Applied to biology: the soul is the form of the body — not a separate substance but the body's principle of organisation and life. This framework, adopted by Aquinas, structured Western philosophy and theology for a millennium and a half, and remains productive in contemporary philosophy of mind and biology.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Civilisation

01
Formal logic — the syllogism and the theory of demonstration in the Organon, creating the first complete system for valid inference. The tool of all subsequent Western science, theology, mathematics, and philosophy for two millennia.
02
Systematic biology — the Historia Animalium and related works classified and described over 500 species with a precision not surpassed until the 17th century. Darwin called him the greatest naturalist who had ever lived.
03
Virtue ethics — the account of human flourishing (eudaimonia) in the Nicomachean Ethics remains the most influential alternative to utilitarian and Kantian moral theory, and the foundation of contemporary virtue ethics.
04
Political science — the Politics surveyed 158 constitutions and classified governments by the number of rulers and whether they rule for the common good or for themselves — the framework still used in introductory political theory.
05
Literary theory — the Poetics defined tragedy, identified its six elements, and introduced catharsis as the emotional function of art. Still the starting point of every serious discussion of drama, narrative, and aesthetic experience.
Ω
…and the very concept of the academic discipline — separating logic, physics, metaphysics, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics into distinct inquiries with distinct methods. The university curriculum is his invention.
04   Problems Addressed

The Insoluble,
Addressed

P.01
Solved the problem of what makes a valid argument valid — by constructing the first formal account of deductive logic in the Prior Analytics, showing that validity depends on form alone, independent of content, and cataloguing all valid syllogistic forms.
Formal Logic — Prior Analytics, c. 350 BCE
Prior Analytics, Books I–II — in the Organon
View Source
P.02
Addressed Plato's problem of how universals relate to particulars — rejecting the theory of separately existing Forms and arguing instead that universals exist only in and through the particular substances that instantiate them, grounding knowledge in observation rather than in recollection of a transcendent realm.
Theory of Universals — Metaphysics, c. 350 BCE
Metaphysics, Books Z, H, Θ (Books VII–IX)
View Source
P.03
Proposed a complete account of what it means to explain something scientifically — the doctrine of four causes — showing that a full explanation of any natural phenomenon requires identifying its matter, form, efficient cause, and final cause, and that the final cause (purpose) is indispensable for understanding living things.
Scientific Explanation — Physics & Parts of Animals, c. 350 BCE
Physics II.3; Parts of Animals I.1
View Source
P.04
Answered the question of what the best human life consists in — arguing against pleasure, honour, and wealth as ultimate ends, and instead identifying eudaimonia (flourishing) as the exercise of reason and virtue over a complete life, embedded in a political community that makes such a life possible.
Ethics — Nicomachean Ethics, c. 350 BCE
Nicomachean Ethics, Book I, Chapters 1–7
View Source
P.05
Explained why tragedy produces pleasure rather than pain — through the concept of catharsis: the audience's pity and fear are aroused by the downfall of a good person through a fatal error (hamartia), and the structured emotional experience of the drama purges or clarifies these feelings, producing a distinctive and valuable form of understanding.

05   Limitations & Errors

Where Even
Aristotle Stumbled

⚠ His physics was almost entirely wrong. He held that heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones (contradicted by Galileo), that the Earth is the stationary centre of the universe, that circular motion is natural for heavenly bodies, and that a vacuum is impossible. These errors, transmitted through Aquinas into scholastic authority, actively retarded the Scientific Revolution for centuries.
⚠ His biology, while astonishing in its observational range, was corrupted by teleological assumptions and male-centric bias. He held that women are defective males, that the male provides the formative principle in reproduction while the female provides only matter, and that menstrual blood is the material cause of the embryo. These errors were accepted as authoritative for almost 2,000 years.
⚠ He defended natural slavery — arguing that some people are by nature suited only to obey and benefit from being directed by rational masters. This doctrine provided philosophical cover for centuries of enslavement, and was used explicitly by European colonisers to justify the subjugation of indigenous peoples. It represents the most morally consequential failure in the history of Western philosophy.
⚠ His logic, while revolutionary, was limited to categorical syllogisms and could not represent relational arguments, mathematical reasoning, or propositional logic with the precision that Frege, Russell, and Peano achieved in the 19th century. The near-sacred authority of Aristotelian logic in medieval Europe actively blocked the development of more powerful logical tools for over a millennium.
⚠ His cosmology included an "unmoved mover" — a divine intellect that moves the heavenly spheres by being the object of their desire, without itself moving or being affected. Aquinas identified this with the Christian God, cementing Aristotelian cosmology into Church doctrine in a way that made heliocentrism theologically dangerous — contributing directly to the persecution of Galileo sixteen centuries later.
