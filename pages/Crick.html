01   Biography

A Life That
Read the Book of Life

Francis Harry Compton Crick was born in Northampton, England, the son of a shoe manufacturer. He studied physics at University College London, but World War II interrupted his doctorate — he spent the war designing naval mines for the Admiralty. After the war he pivoted to biology, drawn by Erwin Schrödinger's provocative 1944 book What Is Life?, which suggested that genetics might be governed by a physical "aperiodic crystal" encoding biological information.

At the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge, Crick met the young American James Watson in 1951. Their collaboration was unconventional — they were assigned to other projects, used other scientists' data, and built physical models out of tin and wire. But on 28 February 1953, they walked into the Eagle pub in Cambridge and announced they had "found the secret of life." The double helix — two antiparallel sugar-phosphate strands wound around each other, connected by specific base pairs (A with T, G with C) — explained both the storage and replication of genetic information in a single elegant structure.

Crick received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1962 with Watson and Maurice Wilkins. He went on to formulate the Central Dogma of molecular biology, propose the adaptor hypothesis that led to the discovery of tRNA, and spend the last 28 years of his life at the Salk Institute studying the neuroscience of consciousness — a problem he pursued with the same restless intensity until his death in 2004.

02   Principles & Models

The Logic
of Heredity

A–T,   G–C   (antiparallel)
Watson-Crick base pairing: adenine bonds only with thymine (2 hydrogen bonds), guanine only with cytosine (3 hydrogen bonds). The specificity of pairing is the molecular basis of heredity and makes semi-conservative replication possible.
DNA → RNA → Protein
The Central Dogma (Crick, 1958): genetic information flows in one direction — from DNA to RNA by transcription, from RNA to protein by translation. Information cannot flow back from protein to nucleic acid. The governing principle of all molecular biology.
64 codons → 20 amino acids
The genetic code — Crick's theoretical framework showed the code must be triplet (3 bases per codon), non-overlapping, and degenerate (multiple codons per amino acid). This framework guided Nirenberg, Khorana, and others in fully cracking the code by 1966.
tRNA : adaptor for codon → aa
The adaptor hypothesis (Crick, 1955): Crick predicted, before any molecular evidence, that a small RNA adaptor molecule must exist to translate nucleotide sequence into amino acid sequence. Transfer RNA was discovered two years later, confirming the prediction exactly.
p = 3.4 Å/bp,   d = 20 Å,   10 bp/turn (B-DNA)
Structural parameters of the B-form DNA double helix — derived from Rosalind Franklin's X-ray diffraction data (Photo 51) and Crick's helical diffraction theory: rise per base pair 3.4 ångströms, helix diameter 20 Å, 10 base pairs per full turn. These numbers, decoded by Crick from the X-ray patterns, confirmed the double helix model and remain the canonical dimensions of DNA.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Biology

01
The double helix — the structure of DNA revealed how genetic information is stored in base sequence and copied through strand separation, transforming biology from a descriptive to a molecular science.
02
The Central Dogma — DNA → RNA → protein: the one-way flow of genetic information that organises all of molecular biology, cell biology, and biotechnology.
03
The adaptor hypothesis — predicted transfer RNA before anyone knew it existed, one of the most successful theoretical predictions in the history of biology.
04
Genetic code theory — established the theoretical constraints (triplet, non-overlapping, degenerate) that guided the experimental cracking of the entire 64-codon code between 1961 and 1966.
05
Neuroscience of consciousness — spent his final three decades at the Salk Institute with Christof Koch developing the Neural Correlates of Consciousness framework, transforming consciousness from philosophy into experimental neuroscience.
🧬
…and the framework that made genetic engineering, PCR, gene sequencing, CRISPR, and the entire biotechnology industry conceptually possible.

P.01

P.03
Proposed the Central Dogma of molecular biology — that information flows from nucleic acid to protein but never from protein back to nucleic acid — providing the organising principle that unified genetics, biochemistry, and cell biology into a single coherent framework.

P.04
Proved that the genetic code is a triplet code — by the elegant frameshift experiment with Brenner and Barnett using bacteriophage T4 mutants, showing that adding or deleting single bases disrupts the reading frame while adding three restores it, confirming codons are three bases long.

Explained the wobble phenomenon — how a single tRNA anticodon can recognise multiple codons differing only in the third position, using non-standard base pairing at the wobble position — resolving the apparent paradox that 61 sense codons are read by far fewer than 61 tRNA molecules.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Crick Stumbled

⚠ Crick and Watson used Rosalind Franklin's X-ray diffraction data — particularly Photo 51 — without her knowledge or direct consent, mediated through their MRC colleague Max Perutz and through Watson seeing the photo via Wilkins. Franklin died in 1958 and could not share the Nobel; the ethical questions about credit remain contested to this day.
⚠ The Central Dogma, while broadly correct, was later found to have exceptions: retroviruses use reverse transcriptase to copy RNA back into DNA, and prions transmit information via protein conformation. Crick later clarified the dogma's precise meaning but the popular version overstated its universality.
⚠ Directed panspermia — the hypothesis Crick proposed with Orgel that life on Earth was seeded by microbes sent deliberately by an alien civilisation — is considered by most biologists a scientifically unfalsifiable speculation with no supporting evidence, regarded as one of his least credible ideas.
⚠ His later work on consciousness — while conceptually important in legitimising the field — has been criticised for over-emphasising the role of the claustrum as the "seat of consciousness," a hypothesis that has not been confirmed and may reflect a search for a single anatomical locus where none exists.
⚠ Crick held strongly reductionist and materialist views about the mind — expressed in his book The Astonishing Hypothesis — which many philosophers and neuroscientists found philosophically naive, dismissing the hard problem of consciousness rather than engaging with it rigorously.
