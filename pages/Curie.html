01   Biography

A Life That
Glowed with Purpose

Maria Sklodowska was born in Warsaw, then under Russian rule, to a family of teachers who nurtured her insatiable curiosity. Denied higher education in Poland, she moved to Paris in 1891, enrolled at the Sorbonne, and graduated top of her class in physics — then again in mathematics.

Working alongside her husband Pierre in a leaky, makeshift shed, she coined the term radioactivity and discovered two new elements — polonium (named for her homeland) and radium — through years of exhausting manual extraction from tonnes of pitchblende ore.

In 1903 she became the first woman to win the Nobel Prize, and in 1911 she won a second — in a different science — making her the first person ever to achieve that distinction. She remains the only person to have Nobel Prizes in both Physics and Chemistry.
  Major Achievements
  • Nobel Prize in Physics (1903) for discovery of radioactivity
  • Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1911) for isolation of radium and polonium
  • Discovery of polonium and radium (1898)

02   Equations & Laws

The Mathematics
of Invisible Forces

A = λN
Radioactive activity equals decay constant times the number of undecayed nuclei — the quantitative heartbeat of radioactivity Curie measured.
N(t) = N₀ e−λt
Radioactive decay law: the number of undecayed atoms decreases exponentially over time, defining the concept of half-life.
t½ = ln2 / λ
Half-life formula: time for half of a radioactive sample to decay — a concept Curie's experimental work made precise for the first time.
Q = ΔE = Δmc²
Nuclear Q-value: energy released in radioactive decay, traceable to mass-energy equivalence — whose physical foundation Curie's radium experiments helped reveal.
I ∝ 1 / r²
Inverse-square radiation law: intensity falls off with the square of distance — a principle central to Curie's precise ionization-chamber measurements of radioactivity.
03   Contributions

What She
Gave Science

01
Coined the term radioactivity and established that it was an atomic property — not a chemical reaction — fundamentally reshaping atomic theory.
02
Discovered polonium and radium, expanding the periodic table and proving that new elements could be found hidden in known ores.
03
Developed techniques to isolate radioactive isotopes, creating the methodology for nuclear chemistry that researchers use to this day.
04
Pioneered medical applications of radiation — radium therapy for tumours became the direct ancestor of modern radiation oncology.
05
Deployed mobile radiography units in WWI, providing front-line X-ray diagnosis that saved countless soldiers' lives.
☢
…and a permanent reordering of what science believed possible for a woman to achieve.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Curie Stumbled

⚠ She underestimated the biological hazard of radiation, carrying test tubes of radioactive isotopes in her pockets and storing them in her desk — her notebooks remain too radioactive to handle safely today.
⚠ Devoted decades to extracting vast quantities of radium for medical use, without building theoretical models to explain why radioactivity occurred at the nuclear level — that fell to Rutherford and Soddy.
⚠ Resisted early quantum mechanical interpretations of atomic decay, preferring classical experimental frameworks even as quantum theory overtook the field in the 1920s.
⚠ Her focus on isolation and measurement left the theoretical explanation of nuclear transmutation — why one element becomes another during decay — to Rutherford and Soddy (1902).
⚠ Chronic exposure to ionising radiation severely damaged her health, ultimately causing aplastic anaemia — she never acknowledged the connection publicly, possibly delaying safety protocols in her own labs.
