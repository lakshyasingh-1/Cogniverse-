01   Biography

The Man Who
Owned Mathematics

Leonhard Euler was born in Basel, Switzerland in 1707, the son of a Calvinist pastor who had studied mathematics under Jakob Bernoulli. He enrolled at the University of Basel at thirteen, took his master's degree at sixteen, and came to the attention of Johann Bernoulli — the greatest mathematician of the age — who recognised something extraordinary and gave the boy private tutorials on Saturday afternoons.

At nineteen he published his first paper. He never stopped. Over a career spanning six decades — first at the St. Petersburg Academy, then at Frederick the Great's Berlin Academy, then back in St. Petersburg — Euler produced 886 books and papers, a body of work so vast that the Opera Omnia collected edition runs to over 80 volumes and is still not complete. He introduced or standardised most of the notation modern mathematics uses: f(x), e, i, π, Σ, Δ.

He lost sight in his right eye in 1738 and went completely blind in 1766. His output barely slowed. Working from memory and dictation, he continued to produce a paper a week until his death in 1783 — reportedly passing away at his desk, chalk in hand, mid-calculation.

02   Equations

The Mathematics
He Left Behind

eiπ + 1 = 0
Euler's identity: five of the most fundamental constants in mathematics — e, i, π, 1, 0 — united in a single equation. Voted the most beautiful formula in mathematics.
eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ
Euler's formula: connects complex exponentials to trigonometry. The single most used result in all of engineering, physics, and signal processing.
∑n=1∞ 1/n² = π²/6
Solution to the Basel problem: a sum that had defeated every mathematician for nearly a century. Euler solved it in 1734 and astonished Europe.
V − E + F = 2
Euler's polyhedron formula: for any convex polyhedron, vertices minus edges plus faces always equals 2. The founding theorem of topology.
d/dt(∂L/∂q̇) − ∂L/∂q = 0
Euler–Lagrange equation: the central equation of classical mechanics and field theory, derived from the calculus of variations that Euler himself founded. Every physical law from optics to general relativity can be written in this form.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Mathematics

01
Graph theory — invented from scratch to solve the Königsberg bridge problem, creating an entirely new branch of mathematics with a single paper in 1736.
02
Modern mathematical notation — standardised e, i, π, f(x), Σ, and Δ, giving mathematics a universal language it had lacked for two thousand years.
03
Calculus of variations — founded the field and derived the Euler–Lagrange equations, the language of all modern theoretical physics.
04
Number theory — proved Fermat's little theorem, introduced the totient function φ(n), and laid the groundwork for modern cryptography and analytic number theory.
05
Fluid mechanics — the Euler equations for inviscid flow remain fundamental to aerodynamics, naval architecture, and atmospheric modelling to this day.
∞
…and notation so universal that students today use Euler's symbols without ever knowing his name.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Euler Stumbled

⚠ Euler's handling of infinite series was often non-rigorous by modern standards — he summed divergent series without justification, producing results that were correct but whose validity was not proved until Cauchy a generation later.
⚠ His derivation of the Basel problem, while correct, relied on an unproven analogy between finite polynomials and infinite trigonometric series — the rigorous proof came only with Weierstrass and Cauchy.
⚠ Euler's fluid equations assume inviscid (frictionless) flow — a simplification that breaks down for real fluids near surfaces, the domain later addressed by Navier and Stokes.
⚠ Despite his enormous number-theoretic output, Euler never proved Fermat's Last Theorem beyond the n=3 case — it remained open for 357 years until Wiles in 1995.
⚠ Working before the 19th-century rigour revolution, Euler had no formal concept of limits, continuity, or convergence — foundations that Cauchy and Weierstrass had to reconstruct beneath his correct results.
