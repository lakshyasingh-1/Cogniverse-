01   Biography

The Woman Who
Photographed Life

Rosalind Elsie Franklin was born on 25 July 1920 in Notting Hill, London, into a prominent Anglo-Jewish family. Intellectually exceptional from childhood, she won a scholarship to Newnham College, Cambridge, graduating in 1941 with a degree in physical chemistry. She completed her doctorate at Cambridge on the microstructure of coals, which became foundational work in industrial chemistry and carbon science.

From 1947 to 1950 she worked in Paris at the Laboratoire Central des Services Chimiques de l'État, where she mastered X-ray crystallography under Jacques Mering — the technique that would define her legacy. She returned to Britain in 1951 to work at King's College London, tasked with applying X-ray diffraction to biological molecules, principally DNA. Within months she had distinguished two distinct forms of DNA — the dry A-form and the hydrated B-form — and had developed the experimental conditions to produce the sharpest diffraction images yet achieved.

In May 1952 she took Photo 51 — an X-ray diffraction image of B-form DNA that immediately revealed a helical structure with a precise pitch, diameter, and repeat. Her careful measurements and detailed calculations, recorded in her lab notebooks, established the sugar-phosphate backbone as external, two-fold symmetry, a 3.4 Å base spacing, and a 34 Å helical pitch with 10 bases per turn. Without her knowledge or consent, her colleague Maurice Wilkins showed Photo 51 to Watson and Crick in January 1953, and her unpublished data were shared with them through the Medical Research Council. Watson and Crick published the double-helix model in April 1953; their paper's acknowledgement of Franklin's contribution was minimal.

Franklin moved to Birkbeck College in 1953, where she produced equally brilliant work on the structure of tobacco mosaic virus and other viruses, co-authoring dozens of papers and establishing the field of structural virology. She died of ovarian cancer on 16 April 1958, aged 37 — almost certainly caused by repeated X-ray exposure. Watson, Crick, and Wilkins received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1962, four years after her death. The Nobel is not awarded posthumously.

"The X-shaped pattern of reflections is the unmistakable fingerprint of a helix. The spacing of the layer lines gives the pitch directly: 34 ångströms per turn. 02   Methods & Measurements

The Mathematics
of the Double Helix

Pitch = 34 Å · Rise/residue = 3.4 Å · n = 10 bp/turn
Franklin's measured B-DNA parameters from Photo 51: a helical pitch of 34 Å, 3.4 Å rise per base pair, and 10 base pairs per complete turn. These three numbers define the geometry of DNA. Watson and Crick used all three directly from her unpublished MRC report.
nλ = 2d · sinθ    (Bragg's Law)
Bragg's Law underlies X-ray crystallography: constructive interference occurs when the path difference 2d·sinθ equals an integer multiple of the wavelength. Franklin's mastery of this technique, with custom-built humidity-controlled cameras, produced diffraction patterns of unprecedented clarity.
I(R,l) ∝ |Jn(2πRr)|² for helical diffraction
Cochran–Crick–Vand helix diffraction theory: the intensity pattern of a helix in X-ray diffraction is described by Bessel functions Jn. The characteristic X-pattern in Photo 51 is the Bessel function signature of a double helix — Franklin read it correctly before Watson and Crick.
Space group: C2 (monoclinic) · a=22.0 Å, b=40.0 Å, c=28.1 Å
Franklin's crystallographic unit cell for A-form DNA: by determining the space group and unit cell dimensions, she deduced that the sugar-phosphate backbone must be on the outside of the helix — a conclusion that directly contradicted all existing models, including Pauling's.
TMV: outer radius = 90 Å · inner channel = 20 Å · 49 subunits/3 turns · RNA runs in helical groove at r = 40 Å
Franklin's tobacco mosaic virus structure (1955–1958): her X-ray analysis of TMV established the precise helical arrangement of protein subunits, located the RNA strand inside the protein coat at a radius of 40 Å, and determined the virus dimensions to the ångström. This work, entirely her own, was presented at an international symposium just weeks before her death — and is considered by structural biologists to be of equivalent originality to the DNA work.
03   Contributions

What She
Gave Science

01
Photo 51 — the clearest X-ray diffraction image of DNA ever taken, immediately revealing the helical structure, pitch, diameter, and base spacing of B-form DNA.
02
A-form / B-form distinction — she was the first to understand that DNA exists in two distinct structural forms depending on hydration, and that B-form was biologically relevant.
03
External backbone — from crystallographic unit cell analysis she determined unambiguously that the phosphate groups must be on the outside of the helix, not the inside as all models assumed.
04
Tobacco mosaic virus structure — her independent structural work at Birkbeck established the complete geometry of TMV, founding the field of structural virology.
05
Carbon science — her doctoral work on coal microstructure and graphitisation remains a foundational reference in materials science, long predating her DNA work.
∞
…and a legacy of precision and rigour that every structural biologist since has had to match.
04   Problems Solved

The Unsolvable,
Solved

P.01
Produced the first unambiguous experimental evidence that DNA is helical — Photo 51's X-shaped diffraction pattern is the direct crystallographic signature of a helix, readable without modelling.
Source: X-Ray Crystallography Proof: Photo 51, May 1952
P.02
Determined the precise helix parameters of B-DNA — pitch 34 Å, rise 3.4 Å, 10 bases per turn — the three numbers Watson and Crick needed to build their model and were unable to determine themselves.
Source: DNA Structure Proof: MRC Report, Feb 1953 (seen by Watson & Crick)
P.03
Proved the sugar-phosphate backbone is external — unit cell analysis of A-form DNA demonstrated that the phosphate groups face outward, demolishing the Pauling triple-helix model and all inside-backbone proposals.
Source: Crystallographic Analysis Proof: Franklin & Gosling, Acta Cryst. (1953)
P.04
Determined the complete structure of tobacco mosaic virus — locating the RNA inside the protein coat at radius 40 Å and fully characterising the helical subunit packing, founding structural virology.
Source: Structural Virology Proof: Franklin et al., Nature (1955, 1956, 1958)
P.05
Characterised the microstructure of graphitising and non-graphitising carbons — her doctoral thesis established the structural basis of carbon materials, work that underpins modern carbon nanotechnology and graphene research.
Source: Materials Science Proof: Franklin, Acta Cryst. (1950, 1951)
05   Context & Injustices

What Science
Did to Her

⚠ Photo 51 and her unpublished MRC report were shown to Watson and Crick without her knowledge or consent in early 1953. Watson later admitted in The Double Helix that seeing Photo 51 was the moment the structure became clear to him.
⚠ Watson, Crick, and Wilkins received the 1962 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the double-helix discovery. The Nobel is not awarded posthumously; Franklin had died in 1958. Whether she would have shared it is debated — but her experimental contribution was foundational and unacknowledged in the prize citation.
⚠ Watson's 1968 memoir The Double Helix portrayed her dismissively as obstructive and unattractive — a characterisation widely condemned by her colleagues and contradicted by the historical record. It shaped public perception of her for decades.
⚠ She worked in isolation at King's College, excluded from the common room and treated as a technical assistant rather than a principal investigator. Wilkins believed she had been assigned to assist him; she believed she had been assigned an independent project. This confusion was never resolved.
⚠ Her death at 37 from ovarian cancer, almost certainly caused by occupational X-ray exposure during a decade of crystallographic work, ended a scientific career whose second act — structural virology — was as brilliant as the first. What she would have achieved in another thirty years is incalculable.
