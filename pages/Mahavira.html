Mahavira

c. 599 BCE — c. 527 BCE

The 24th Tirthankara — he renounced the world at thirty, achieved omniscience through twelve years of austerity, and gave humanity the most radical philosophy of non-violence ever articulated.

01   Biography

A Life of
Radical Renunciation

Vardhamana — later honoured as Mahavira, the Great Hero — was born around 599 BCE in Kundagrama, near present-day Vaishali in Bihar, India, into the Kshatriya warrior caste. His father was Siddhartha, a chief of the Licchavi clan; his mother Trishala, a queen of noble lineage. From his earliest years he is said to have displayed extraordinary fearlessness, compassion, and philosophical temperament — the Jain tradition holds that even in the womb he resolved to minimise movement lest he disturb his mother.

At thirty, having lived as a householder and witnessed the suffering woven into every layer of worldly life, Mahavira renounced his wealth, family, and clothing — adopting the life of an unclothed ascetic. For twelve years and five months he wandered the Gangetic plain in total silence, practising extreme austerity, fasting for weeks at a time, enduring cold, heat, insect bites, and the hostility of villagers without retaliation or complaint. He accepted no shelter, owned nothing, harmed no living being — not an insect, not a microorganism if he could help it.

At forty-two, meditating under a Sal tree on the banks of the river Rijupalika, he attained Kevala Jnana — omniscience, complete and perfect knowledge of all things past, present, and future. For thirty years thereafter he taught across the kingdoms of northeastern India, establishing the philosophical system that synthesised and radicalised the earlier Jain tradition: an absolute ethics of non-violence (Ahimsa), a sophisticated epistemology of plural perspectives (Anekāntavāda), and a metaphysics in which the soul's liberation depends on its own effort, entirely independent of any god or creator.

He died — or in Jain terms, attained Moksha, liberation from the cycle of rebirth — at Pavapuri around 527 BCE. His teachings were preserved by his eleven principal disciples (Ganadharas) and form the canonical Agamas of Jainism. His philosophical legacy shaped Gandhi's doctrine of non-violent resistance, influenced Buddhism, and remains one of the most rigorously worked-out ethical systems in the history of human thought.
  Major Teachings
  • Ahimsa — absolute non-violence toward all living beings
  • Anekāntavāda — the doctrine of many-sidedness
  • Syādvāda — seven-fold predication logic
  • Aparigraha — non-possessiveness as spiritual law
  • Satya — absolute truthfulness
  • Karma as a physical substance binding the soul
  • Moksha through self-effort, no divine intermediary
02   The Five Great Vows

The Pañca Mahāvratas —
Five Absolute Renunciations

Vow I
Ahimsa
अहिंसा — Non-Violence
The renunciation of all violence — in thought, word, and deed — toward every living being, from humans to microscopic organisms. The most absolute formulation of non-harm in any philosophical tradition. Not a guideline but an unconditional prohibition.
Vow II
Satya
सत्य — Truthfulness
The renunciation of all false speech. Truth is not merely a social virtue but a metaphysical imperative: untruth injures reality itself. Mahavira subordinated Satya to Ahimsa — if truth would cause harm, silence is required.
Vow III
Asteya
अस्तेय — Non-Stealing
The renunciation of taking anything not freely given — including attention, credit, or advantage. Extends far beyond property to any appropriation of what belongs to another being, including the earth's resources beyond what sustains bare life.
Vow IV
Brahmacharya
ब्रह्मचर्य — Celibacy
The renunciation of sensual pleasure. Not as punishment but as the recognition that desire binds the soul to the cycle of rebirth. Liberation requires the dissolution of all attachment, of which erotic attachment is the most tenacious.
Vow V
Aparigraha
अपरिग्रह — Non-Possessiveness
The renunciation of all possessiveness — the deepest of the five. Mahavira himself possessed nothing: no clothing, no bowl, no shelter. Aparigraha extends to psychological possession — the need to own outcomes, opinions, relationships, and identity. It is the philosophical root of all five vows, the recognition that the self that clings to things is the very self that must be dissolved for liberation to occur.
03   Philosophical Doctrines

The Logic of
Many-Sidedness

Anekāntavāda: Reality has ∞ aspects
The doctrine of non-absolutism: no single perspective can capture the full nature of any reality. Every philosophical claim is true from some standpoint and false from another. This is not relativism — it is the recognition that reality is inexhaustibly complex and that every finite perspective sees a real but partial truth.
Syādvāda: "Syāt" + 7 predicates
Seven-fold predication: every statement about reality must be qualified by "syāt" (perhaps, in some respect). The seven forms: (1) perhaps it is; (2) perhaps it is not; (3) perhaps it is and is not; (4) perhaps it is indescribable; (5–7) combinations. A formal logic of epistemic humility 2,500 years before analytic philosophy.
Karma = material substance + Jīva
Mahavira taught karma not as abstract moral accounting but as a literal fine-grained matter (karma-pudgala) that adheres to the soul (Jīva) through passionate action, obscuring its innate omniscience and bliss. Liberation is the removal of all accumulated karma through austerity and right conduct.
Moksha: Jīva − Karma = Siddha
Liberation equation: when all karma is shed, the soul (Jīva) attains the state of Siddha — pure, omniscient, liberated consciousness that rises to the apex of the universe and abides there in perfect, motionless bliss, never returning to the cycle of rebirth.
Ahimsa Paramo Dharma — अहिंसा परमो धर्म
Non-violence is the supreme religion — the axiomatic foundation of Mahavira's entire ethical system. This single principle, taken with absolute seriousness, generates all the other vows and all of Jain practice: the vegetarianism, the care not to harm insects, the wearing of masks by monks, the refusal of agriculture (which injures root vegetables and soil organisms), and the embrace of the most radical asceticism in human history. Gandhi called this the greatest gift India had given the world.
04   Contributions

What He
Gave Philosophy

01
Anekāntavāda — the first systematic philosophical doctrine of perspectivism: the recognition that every claim is true from some standpoint, anticipating by 2,500 years debates central to modern epistemology and philosophy of language.
02
Ahimsa as absolute principle — transformed non-violence from a cultural preference into a rigorously argued philosophical imperative, applicable to every living being without exception or degree.
03
Syādvāda — a seven-valued predicate logic that formalises epistemic humility; one of the earliest examples of multi-valued logic in the history of philosophy, predating Łukasiewicz by two millennia.
04
Karma as physical matter — a materialist theory of moral causation, in which karma is a real substance rather than a metaphor, allowing a causal account of how actions bind and liberate the soul.
05
Atheistic liberation theology — a complete soteriology (theory of liberation) with no role for a creator god; the soul liberates itself through its own effort, establishing the most rigorous philosophical case for self-reliance in the history of religion.
∞
…and the philosophical foundation for every non-violent movement in human history, from Gandhi to Mandela.
05   Problems Addressed

The Unanswerable,
Answered

P.01
The problem of violence in existence — how can a living being survive and act without causing harm? Mahavira's answer was graduated: minimise harm at every level, accept what is unavoidable, and calibrate action by the degree of consciousness of the beings involved.
Domain: Ethics Teaching: Ahimsa Gradation
P.02
The problem of dogmatism — how can competing philosophical schools all claim absolute truth? Mahavira's Anekāntavāda resolved this: each school grasps a real aspect of a reality too complex for any single perspective to capture wholly.
Domain: Epistemology Teaching: Anekāntavāda
P.03
The problem of suffering and its cause — why do souls suffer? Mahavira's answer: because karma — attracted by passion and violence — obscures the soul's innate omniscience and bliss. Remove karma through austerity and right conduct, and liberation follows necessarily.
Domain: Metaphysics Teaching: Karma Theory
P.04
The problem of divine authority — can liberation depend on a god's grace? Mahavira's answer was unequivocal: no. The soul is inherently free and omniscient; its bondage is self-created through karma; its liberation is self-achieved through right knowledge, right faith, and right conduct.
Domain: Soteriology Teaching: Triratna — Three Jewels
P.05
The problem of possession and identity — what is the self that clings to things, and why does clinging cause suffering? Mahavira's Aparigraha doctrine: possession is not a legal or economic category but a psychological one — the self's compulsive need to extend itself into objects, relationships, and ideas, which generates the very anxiety it seeks to resolve.
Domain: Psychology & Ethics Teaching: Aparigraha
06   Tensions & Critiques

Where the Philosophy
Faces Challenges

⚠ The rigour of Ahimsa creates an impossible standard for lay life — strict Jain ascetics cannot cook (fire kills organisms), farm (ploughing harms soil creatures), or even walk carelessly. Critics argue that an ethics that can only be fully practised by renunciants is not a complete ethics for human society.
⚠ Anekāntavāda risks self-refutation: if all views are partially true, is the view that "all views are partially true" itself only partially true? The doctrine must either claim a privileged standpoint — which contradicts it — or be subject to its own relativising.
⚠ The karma doctrine, while philosophically sophisticated, relies on a metaphysical entity — the soul (Jīva) — whose existence cannot be empirically verified. Buddhist critics argued that Mahavira's substantialist soul contradicts the observable reality of impermanence and interdependence.
⚠ The historical record is sparse and filtered through centuries of hagiographic tradition. Distinguishing Mahavira's original teachings from the accretions of the Jain canonical tradition — compiled centuries after his death — is a problem analogous to separating the historical Jesus from the Gospels.
⚠ The radical asceticism Mahavira embodied and recommended — including the practice of Sallekhana (fasting unto death as the ultimate renunciation) — has been criticised as life-denying rather than life-honouring, and in modern legal terms has been challenged in Indian courts as a form of suicide.
