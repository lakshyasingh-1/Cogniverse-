01   Biography

The Man Who
Ordered the Elements

Dmitri Ivanovich Mendeleev was born on 8 February 1834 in Tobolsk, Siberia — the youngest of somewhere between eleven and seventeen children (accounts differ) of a schoolteacher father who went blind shortly after his birth. His mother, determined that her most gifted child receive a proper education, walked him across Siberia and the Ural mountains to St. Petersburg after his father's death, a journey of over a thousand miles. She died shortly after enrolling him at the Main Pedagogical Institute.

He graduated first in his class, taught briefly in Odessa, then earned his master's degree and eventually a professorship at the University of St. Petersburg. In 1868, tasked with writing a chemistry textbook, he found no existing organisation of the elements satisfactory. He began arranging cards, one per element, by atomic weight, searching for a pattern. On 1 March 1869, the pattern crystallised: properties of elements repeat periodically as atomic weight increases. He presented his periodic table to the Russian Chemical Society the same day.

What made it extraordinary was not just the organisation — it was the gaps. Mendeleev left deliberate blanks for elements he was certain existed but had not yet been discovered, and predicted their properties with remarkable specificity. Within fifteen years, gallium (1875), scandium (1879), and germanium (1886) were all discovered, matching his predictions almost exactly. Science had never seen anything like it: a theory that predicted not just what was known, but what was unknown.

The Periodic Law: the physical and chemical properties of the elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights. The most powerful organising principle in all of chemistry.
PV = nRT
Ideal gas law — Mendeleev made significant contributions to the study of gases and their deviations from ideal behaviour, working toward understanding the critical point of substances.
Apredicted ≈ Aobserved
The predictive power of the table: Mendeleev predicted the atomic weight of eka-silicon (germanium) as 72; the observed value was 72.6. For eka-aluminium (gallium): predicted 68, observed 69.7.
Z → Z+1  :  Δ(properties) periodic
Modern restatement: as atomic number Z increases by one, chemical properties repeat with a period determined by electron shell filling — the quantum mechanical foundation Mendeleev never knew but perfectly anticipated.
Group · Period · Block → [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s² 6p² (Pb)
Electron configuration notation — the quantum mechanical language that explains why Mendeleev's table works. Every element's position encodes its electron configuration; elements in the same group share the same valence shell structure. Mendeleev intuited this entire framework from macroscopic chemical behaviour alone, forty years before the electron was understood.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
The Periodic Table — the single most powerful organising framework in chemistry, which reduced thousands of scattered chemical facts to one coherent, predictive system.
02
The Periodic Law — the explicit statement that elemental properties repeat periodically with atomic weight, giving chemistry its first true law analogous to those of physics.
03
Predictive chemistry — the radical idea that gaps in the table implied undiscovered elements, with specific predictions of their properties: a new mode of scientific reasoning.
04
Critical temperature of liquids — he independently determined that every gas has a temperature above which it cannot be liquefied regardless of pressure, foundational to thermodynamics.
05
Principles of Chemistry — his two-volume textbook was the standard chemistry text in Russia for a generation, training an entire cohort of Russian scientists.
∞
…and a framework so robust it absorbed a century of new physics — quantum mechanics, relativity, nuclear structure — without breaking.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Mendeleev Stumbled

⚠ Mendeleev organised by atomic weight, not atomic number — causing a few elements (tellurium/iodine, cobalt/nickel) to appear out of order. It took Moseley's 1913 X-ray work to establish atomic number as the true organising principle.
⚠ He had no explanation for why the periodicity existed — the quantum mechanical answer (electron shell filling) lay forty years in the future. His law was empirical genius without theoretical foundation.
⚠ He fiercely resisted the existence of the electron and the theory of electrolytic dissociation proposed by Arrhenius — dismissing Arrhenius's Nobel Prize-winning work as unphysical right up to his death.
⚠ He initially struggled to place the rare earth elements (lanthanides) in the table — a problem not properly resolved until the understanding of f-block electron configurations in the 20th century.
⚠ He lost the 1906 Nobel Prize by a single committee vote — awarded instead to Henri Moissan for isolating fluorine. He died in 1907 without ever receiving the prize his periodic table almost certainly deserved.
