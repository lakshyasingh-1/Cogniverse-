01   Biography

A Mind That
Ordered the Cosmos

Isaac Newton was born prematurely on Christmas Day, 1643, in Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire. Raised by his grandmother after his mother remarried, he showed little early promise — until he enrolled at Trinity College, Cambridge, and the universe opened up to him.

During the Great Plague of 1665–66, Cambridge shut down and Newton retreated to Woolsthorpe. In those eighteen months of isolation, he invented calculus, formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, and discovered that white light is a spectrum of colours. Science calls this his 'Annus Mirabilis' — two years before Einstein's.

His 1687 masterpiece, Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica, is arguably the greatest scientific work ever published, founding classical mechanics in a single sweep. He later served as Warden — then Master — of the Royal Mint, and as President of the Royal Society, cementing an extraordinary dual career in science and public life.

02   Equations

The Mathematics
of Motion & Force

F = ma
Second Law of Motion: force equals mass times acceleration. The equation that defines classical mechanics.
F = G · m₁m₂ / r²
Universal Gravitation: every mass attracts every other with a force inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
v = u + at
Kinematic equation of uniformly accelerated motion — foundational to all of classical dynamics and engineering.
n = sin(i) / sin(r)
Snell's Law recast: Newton used this in his optical work to explain refraction and the splitting of white light into its constituent colours.
d/dx[xⁿ] = nxⁿ⁻¹
Power rule of differentiation — the most-used result from the calculus Newton invented (his "method of fluxions"), underpinning all of modern mathematics and physics.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
Three Laws of Motion — the cornerstone of classical mechanics, still used in every engineering calculation on Earth and in space.
02
Universal Gravitation — a single equation uniting terrestrial and celestial mechanics, explaining both falling apples and planetary orbits.
03
Calculus (method of fluxions) — independently invented alongside Leibniz; the mathematical language of change that drives all modern science.
04
Optics & the spectrum — proved white light is a mixture of colours and designed the first practical reflecting telescope.
05
Principia Mathematica (1687) — the single most influential book in the history of natural science, unifying physics under one mathematical framework.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Newton Stumbled

⚠ Classical mechanics breaks down at speeds approaching light — superseded by Einstein's special relativity in 1905.
⚠ Gravity described how objects attract but offered no explanation of why — Newton himself conceded "hypotheses non fingo."
⚠ Corpuscular (particle) theory of light could not explain diffraction or interference — wave theory, and later quantum mechanics, proved correct.
⚠ Newtonian mechanics fails at atomic and subatomic scales — the domain of quantum mechanics, which Newton could not have foreseen.
⚠ Devoted his final decades to alchemy and biblical chronology — vast intellectual energy poured into pursuits that yielded nothing of lasting scientific value.
