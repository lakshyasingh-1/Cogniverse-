01   Biography

The Man Who
Knew Nothing

Socrates was born around 470 BC in Athens to Sophroniscus, a stonemason, and Phaenarete, a midwife — a detail he used to describe his own method: as his mother delivered bodies, he delivered minds of ideas they did not know they were carrying. He served with distinction as a hoplite soldier in the Peloponnesian War, demonstrating physical courage at Potidaea, Amphipolis, and Delium. But it was in the agora, the marketplace of Athens, that he conducted his true work.

He wrote nothing. Everything we know of him comes through others, primarily the dialogues of his student Plato, the memoirs of Xenophon, and the satirical portrait in Aristophanes' comedy The Clouds. This presents an irreducible problem — the "Socratic problem" — of separating the historical man from the literary character. What seems consistent across sources is his method: the elenchus, or cross-examination. He would approach citizens, poets, craftsmen, and statesmen who claimed expertise, and by gentle questioning expose that they did not actually know what they thought they knew. He believed this was a service to Athens, inspired by the Oracle at Delphi's declaration that no man was wiser than Socrates — which he interpreted to mean only that he alone knew he knew nothing.

In 399 BC he was tried before a jury of five hundred Athenians on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth of Athens. He refused to flee, refused to grovel, refused to propose a sufficient penalty. He was convicted by a narrow margin and executed by hemlock, dying in the company of his friends while discussing the immortality of the soul — a death so philosophically composed that it became the founding image of the philosopher as one who lives and dies by reason alone.
  Major Contributions
  • Invented the Socratic method of philosophical inquiry
  • Founded moral philosophy as a rigorous discipline
  • Established the examined life as the philosophical ideal
  • Taught Plato, who taught Aristotle — the lineage of Western thought
  • Defined virtue as knowledge and vice as ignorance
  • Introduced the concept of the psyche (soul) as the seat of moral character
  • His trial and death shaped the idea of the philosopher's duty to truth

Claim → Elenchus → Aporia → Deeper Inquiry
The Socratic Method: accept a claim, draw out its implications through questioning, reveal the contradiction or ignorance at its root (aporia), and use the resulting discomfort as the engine of genuine inquiry. The world's first systematic philosophical procedure.
Virtue = Knowledge  ·  Vice = Ignorance
His most radical ethical thesis: no one does wrong willingly. Wrongdoing is always a failure of understanding — if one truly knew the good, one would do it. Moral failure is an epistemic failure. Ethics and epistemology are inseparable.
"I know that I know nothing"
Socratic irony — the paradox at the heart of his philosophy. Genuine wisdom begins with the acknowledgment of ignorance. The unexamined assumption is more dangerous than acknowledged ignorance. This epistemological humility is itself a form of knowledge.
Care of Soul > Care of Body or Wealth
His most persistent moral exhortation: the psyche is what we truly are, and its condition — its virtue — is what matters above all else. Wealth, reputation, and comfort that come at the cost of the soul's integrity are not gains but losses.
The Examined Life  ·  "ὁ δὲ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς ἀνθρώπῳ"
"The unexamined life is not worth living" — spoken at his own trial, when he refused to accept exile as a penalty because it would have required giving up philosophy. The statement is not a truism but a provocation: most human lives, spent in the pursuit of money, pleasure, and status without reflection on what is good, are in some meaningful sense not fully human lives at all. It is the founding sentence of the Western philosophical tradition.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Philosophy

01
The Socratic Method — the elenctic technique of inquiry through questioning, still the foundational pedagogical tool of philosophy, law, and medicine two and a half millennia later.
02
The turn to ethics — he redirected philosophy from cosmology and natural science (the Pre-Socratics) to the question of how one ought to live. He made the human moral situation the central problem of philosophy.
03
The concept of the psyche as moral locus — he was the first to argue that the soul is not merely the animating principle of the body but the seat of moral character, and that its care is the supreme obligation of every person.
04
The idea of universal definitions — his insistence on finding what courage, piety, and justice are in themselves (not just examples of them) launched the entire tradition of conceptual analysis and abstract thought.
05
The intellectual lineage — through Plato and then Aristotle, Socrates stands at the origin of the entire Western philosophical tradition: logic, metaphysics, ethics, politics, and epistemology all trace back to his conversations.
Ψ
…and a model of intellectual integrity — that one must follow the argument wherever it leads, even to one's own condemnation, and that death is preferable to the abandonment of philosophy.

Confronted

P.01
Exposed the false knowledge of the Sophists — the professional teachers who claimed to sell virtue and wisdom for money. Through cross-examination he demonstrated that their supposed expertise was rhetorical skill masquerading as genuine understanding, distinguishing persuasion from truth.

P.02
Established that piety, courage, and justice cannot be defined by listing examples — they must have a universal essence. His demand for definitions launched the philosophical problem of universals that occupied Plato, Aristotle, and the medieval scholastics for two millennia.

P.03
Resolved the question of civil disobedience — in the Crito, imprisoned and facing death, he argued that escaping would undermine the laws of Athens whose authority he had benefited from his entire life. He chose to obey an unjust sentence rather than destabilise just institutions.

Demonstrated that virtue cannot simply be taught as a body of knowledge — the Meno's paradox of learning ("how can you seek what you don't know?") and his midwife analogy proposed instead that philosophical education is a process of drawing out truths already latent in the mind.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Socrates Stumbled

⚠ The Socratic method is devastatingly effective at destroying claimed knowledge but weak at constructing positive doctrine. He was the supreme critic but left almost no systematic account of what he actually believed virtue, justice, or the good life consisted in.
⚠ His equation of virtue with knowledge has been challenged ever since Aristotle: people often know what is right and do wrong anyway (akrasia — weakness of will). The purely intellectualist account of moral failure seems to leave no room for passion, habit, or character.
⚠ He accepted without question the institution of slavery, the exclusion of women from political and philosophical life, and the deep class hierarchies of Athenian democracy. His celebrated universalism about reason coexisted with the profound social prejudices of his time.
⚠ His complete reliance on oral dialogue meant he left no writings. Everything we have is mediated — primarily through Plato, who became increasingly his own philosopher. The "Socratic problem" of separating the man from his literary avatar is arguably insoluble.
⚠ His method could be experienced as humiliation rather than illumination. Aristophanes lampooned him; the Athenians ultimately killed him. His habit of publicly exposing the ignorance of prominent citizens made him enemies — and raises questions about whether philosophical antagonism is always a productive form of engagement.
