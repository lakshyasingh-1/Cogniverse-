01   Biography

A Life That
Computed the Future

Alan Mathison Turing was born in London, the son of a civil servant stationed in India. From childhood he showed an extraordinary capacity for independent mathematical thought — teaching himself calculus from first principles, writing on the science of relativity at fifteen without having studied it. He studied mathematics at King's College, Cambridge, where he was elected a Fellow at 22, and earned his PhD at Princeton under Alonzo Church.

In 1936 he published "On Computable Numbers" — a paper that defined what it means for a problem to be solvable by a mechanical procedure. The abstract device at its centre, the Turing Machine, became the theoretical foundation of every computer ever built. In 1939 he joined the codebreakers at Bletchley Park, where he designed the Bombe — an electromechanical machine that cracked the Nazi Enigma cipher and, historians estimate, shortened World War II by two to four years.

After the war he designed one of the first stored-program computers (the ACE), wrote the first chess program, and in 1950 published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" — which proposed the Turing Test and founded the field of artificial intelligence. In 1952 he was prosecuted for homosexuality, chemically castrated by court order, and stripped of his security clearance. He died in 1954 of cyanide poisoning. He was 41. The British government formally apologised in 2009; he was pardoned in 2013.

02   Theorems & Models

The Logic That
Built the Digital Age

M = (Q, Γ, b, Σ, δ, q₀, F)
The Turing Machine — a 7-tuple of states, tape alphabet, blank symbol, input symbols, transition function, start state, and accepting states. Any computable function can be computed by one.
HALT(M, w) is undecidable
The Halting Problem: no general algorithm can determine, for every program M and input w, whether M will halt or run forever. Turing's proof by diagonalisation set the limits of computation itself.
∂a/∂t = F(a,b) + Da∇²a
Turing's reaction-diffusion equation for morphogenesis — describing how two chemical morphogens interacting with different diffusion rates spontaneously produce the patterns of spots, stripes, and whorls seen in animal coats.
P(M passes | M is machine) → 1
The Turing Test (formalised): if a human interrogator cannot reliably distinguish a machine from a human in text conversation, the machine may be said to exhibit intelligence — Turing's operational definition of AI.
UTM simulates any TM M on input w  ≡  U(⟨M,w⟩)
The Universal Turing Machine — a single machine U that reads a description of any other Turing Machine M and its input w, then simulates M's computation exactly. This is the conceptual blueprint of the stored-program computer: hardware that executes arbitrary software.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Civilisation

01
The Turing Machine and computability theory — formalising the notion of algorithm and defining the mathematical boundary between the solvable and the unsolvable, forever.
02
Breaking Enigma at Bletchley Park — the Bombe machine decrypted millions of Nazi messages, giving the Allies decisive intelligence advantages at sea, on land, and in the air throughout WWII.
03
The Universal Turing Machine — the theoretical ancestor of every general-purpose computer: one machine that executes any program by reading its description, not by being rewired.
04
Artificial intelligence — "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950) posed the question machines could think, proposed the Turing Test, and launched AI as a serious scientific discipline.
05
Mathematical biology — his 1952 paper on morphogenesis showed that the complex patterns of life arise from simple chemical reaction-diffusion equations, founding the field of computational developmental biology.
01
…and every smartphone, cloud server, neural network, and digital device on Earth, which runs on the architecture he conceived before any of them existed.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Turing Stumbled

⚠ The Turing Test, while historically pivotal, is now widely criticised as an insufficient criterion for intelligence — a machine can pass it through sophisticated pattern matching without any genuine understanding, as the Chinese Room argument illustrates.
⚠ His ACE computer design — the most detailed of its era — was so far ahead of engineering reality that NPL engineers found it impractical to build. Bureaucratic delays meant it was never fully realised in his lifetime, ceding the first working machine to Manchester and Cambridge.
⚠ Turing's morphogenesis model, while conceptually revolutionary, used simplifying assumptions about linear reaction kinetics that limit its biological accuracy. Modern developmental biology requires far more complex gene regulatory networks than his two-morphogen system.
⚠ He did not develop a theory of computational complexity — the question of not just what can be computed, but at what cost in time and memory. This gap, left to Cook, Karp, and others in the 1970s, produced the P vs NP problem, still unresolved today.
⚠ The circumstances of his death — cyanide poisoning, 16 months after his conviction — remain contested. The coroner ruled suicide, but the evidence is ambiguous; some historians argue it may have been accidental. We will likely never know. What is certain is that the world lost him at 41 — long before he was finished.
