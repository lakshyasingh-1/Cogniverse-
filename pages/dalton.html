01   Biography

A Life That
Weighed the Invisible

John Dalton was born in Eaglesfield, Cumberland, into a Quaker family of modest means. His formal education ended at twelve, yet his intellectual gifts were unmistakable: by the age of fifteen he was teaching at the village school, and at nineteen he moved to Manchester, where he would spend the remainder of his long life, quietly dismantling and rebuilding the foundations of chemistry.

Working at the Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society — an institution he joined in 1794 and would eventually lead as president — Dalton pursued an extraordinarily broad programme of research. He studied meteorology, measured the aurora borealis, investigated colour blindness (a condition he himself had, which was long called Daltonism in his honour), and formulated the law of partial pressures of gases.

But his supreme contribution came between 1803 and 1808 with the publication of A New System of Chemical Philosophy. Drawing on careful measurements of the ratios in which elements combine, Dalton proposed the first quantitative atomic theory: matter is composed of indivisible atoms; all atoms of a given element are identical in mass; compounds form when atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios. He compiled the first table of atomic weights — crude by modern standards, but revolutionary in concept. Every atom in every molecule in every equation written since is Dalton's intellectual descendant.

02   Laws & Principles

The Grammar
of the Elements

Ptotal = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + … + Pₙ
Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures (1801): the total pressure exerted by a mixture of gases equals the sum of the pressures each gas would exert alone. It underpins anaesthesiology, atmospheric science, and industrial gas engineering.
A + B → AₘBₙ   (m, n ∈ ℤ⁺)
Law of Multiple Proportions (1803): when two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in ratios of small whole numbers — direct evidence for discrete atoms.
w(A) / w(B) = constant
Law of Definite Proportions (Dalton's atomic reinterpretation of Proust): every pure compound contains its constituent elements in fixed, definite mass ratios — because atoms always combine in the same whole-number ratio.
Ar(H) = 1,   Ar(O) ≈ 7,   Ar(N) ≈ 5
Dalton's first atomic weight table (1803) — setting hydrogen as the unit and assigning relative atomic masses to other elements by measuring combining ratios. His values contained errors (O should be 8 vs H, not 7), but the concept was revolutionary and structurally correct.
PV = nRT   (ideal gas, Dalton's partial pressure as foundation)
Ideal Gas Law — Dalton's law of partial pressures was the key empirical stepping stone from which Avogadro, Gay-Lussac, and later Clausius built kinetic gas theory and the ideal gas law. Every pressure calculation in chemistry, engineering, and medicine traces back to Dalton's 1801 measurements of gas mixtures above water.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
Atomic Theory — proposed that all matter is made of indivisible atoms, that atoms of each element have a characteristic mass, and that chemical reactions are rearrangements of atoms. This is the conceptual bedrock of all chemistry.
02
Atomic weights — the first quantitative table assigning relative masses to elements. Though imprecise, it made chemistry a measurable, predictive science and enabled Mendeleev's periodic table seven decades later.
03
Law of Partial Pressures — showed that each gas in a mixture behaves independently, exerting its own pressure proportional to its molar fraction. Foundational to respiratory physiology, meteorology, and chemical engineering.
04
Law of Multiple Proportions — the empirical key that unlocked the atom: when two elements form two different compounds, the mass ratios are always in simple integers, proving atoms are discrete and indivisible.
05
Colour blindness research — the first systematic scientific description of red-green colour blindness (1794), published decades before its physiological basis was understood, establishing it as a hereditary condition and not a defect of the eye's humours.
●
…and the unifying idea that chemistry is not a collection of recipes but a single discipline governed by the properties of atoms — transforming it into a predictive science.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Dalton Stumbled

⚠ His atomic weights were systematically wrong because he assumed the simplest possible formulas — water as HO (not H₂O), ammonia as NH (not NH₃). This error cascaded through his entire weight table. Avogadro's hypothesis (1811) would have corrected it, but Dalton rejected it for decades.
⚠ He dismissed Avogadro's hypothesis — that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules — because it conflicted with his belief that atoms of the same element repel each other and cannot form diatomic molecules (O₂, H₂, N₂). This was wrong, and it delayed resolution of the atomic weight problem by 50 years.
⚠ His Rule of Greatest Simplicity — assuming compounds take the simplest atom ratio (1:1) unless evidence forces otherwise — was a heuristic that led him astray repeatedly. It was philosophically reasonable but empirically unjustified and caused systematic errors throughout his work.
⚠ His conception of atoms as indivisible, structureless spheres was eventually overturned by Thomson's discovery of the electron (1897), Rutherford's nuclear atom (1911), and quantum mechanics. The atom Dalton envisioned does not exist — yet his key insights about discrete mass, combining ratios, and conservation survived the revolution intact.
⚠ His explanation of colour blindness — that it arose from a discolouration of the vitreous humour in the eye — was incorrect. After his death, examination of his preserved eyes showed his retina was normal; the cause is a genetic deficiency in cone photoreceptors. His hypothesis was wrong, but the characterisation and hereditary observation were correct.
