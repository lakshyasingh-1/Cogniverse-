01   Biography

A Life That
Branched All Others

Charles Robert Darwin was born in Shrewsbury, Shropshire, the fifth of six children of a wealthy doctor and financier. He was an indifferent student — sent first to Edinburgh to study medicine and then to Cambridge to become a clergyman — but his true passion was natural history. He collected beetles with fanatic intensity and fell under the influence of the botanist John Stevens Henslow, who secured him the position that changed the world.

From 1831 to 1836, Darwin sailed aboard HMS Beagle as the ship's unofficial naturalist on a voyage around the South American coast and across the Pacific. The Galápagos Islands were not his only revelation — the whole voyage was a continuous education in geological change, animal distribution, and the astonishing diversity of life. By the time he returned to England, he suspected the fixity of species was wrong. By 1838, reading Malthus on population pressure, he grasped the mechanism: natural selection.

He sat on the idea for twenty years — accumulating evidence, worrying about the storm his theory would cause — until Alfred Russel Wallace independently arrived at the same conclusion in 1858. Jolted into action, Darwin published On the Origin of Species in November 1859. All 1,250 copies sold on the first day. It remains the most consequential book in the history of biology, and perhaps in all of science. He spent the rest of his life at Down House in Kent, continuing to work prodigiously on earthworms, orchids, barnacles, and sexual selection — never fully recovering from the chronic illness that plagued him from the voyage onwards.

02   Principles & Laws

The Mechanics
of Life's Diversity

Fitness = Survival × Reproduction
Natural selection — individuals with heritable traits that improve survival and reproduction leave more offspring. Over generations, those traits increase in frequency. The engine of all evolutionary change, requiring only variation, heritability, and differential reproduction.
Δp = p·q·(p·w₁₁ + q·w₁₂ − w̄) / w̄
Change in allele frequency under selection (Hardy-Weinberg with selection) — the mathematical formalisation of natural selection Darwin described verbally. Allele p increases when its carriers' average fitness exceeds the population mean fitness.
All life ← Universal Common Ancestor
Common descent — Darwin's most radical claim: every organism on Earth is descended from a single common ancestor. The tree of life is one tree. Confirmed overwhelmingly by comparative genetics, molecular biology, and the universality of the genetic code.
Speciation: gene flow → 0 → divergence
Speciation by geographic isolation: when populations are separated long enough that gene flow ceases, natural selection and drift cause divergence until the populations can no longer interbreed — producing new species from one. Darwin observed the pattern; Dobzhansky and Mayr formalised it.
rB > C   (Hamilton's Rule, foreshadowed by Darwin)
Hamilton's Rule for kin selection — Darwin was troubled by altruism and sterile castes in social insects, which seemed to contradict natural selection. He proposed that selection acts on the family, not the individual — precisely anticipating Hamilton's 1964 formalisation. An altruistic act evolves when the benefit B to the recipient, discounted by their genetic relatedness r, exceeds the cost C to the actor. Darwin's qualitative insight preceded the mathematics by 105 years.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Biology

01
Natural selection — the mechanism by which heritable variation, combined with differential reproductive success, produces adaptation over time. The central organising principle of all biology.
02
Common descent — the demonstration that all life shares a universal ancestor, unified by branching genealogy. Confirmed by every subsequent discovery in genetics, genomics, and palaeontology.
03
Sexual selection — a second evolutionary mechanism driven by competition for mates, explaining extravagant traits (peacock tails, bird-of-paradise plumage) that reduce survival but increase reproductive success.
04
Coral reef theory — proposed that coral atolls form as volcanic islands subside, leaving fringing reefs that become barrier reefs and finally atolls — confirmed by deep drilling through Pacific atolls a century later.
05
The role of earthworms in soil formation — his last book showed that earthworms are the primary agents of topsoil generation, founding soil ecology and demonstrating that immense geological change can arise from tiny, cumulative actions.
🌿
…and the unification of all life under a single explanatory framework — removing teleology from biology and placing it firmly among the natural sciences.

P.03

Accounted for the formation of coral atolls — a geological puzzle of his age — by proposing that fringing reefs, barrier reefs, and atolls are successive stages of the same process as a volcanic island subsides, with coral growing upward to stay within the photic zone.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Darwin Stumbled

⚠ Darwin had no mechanism for heredity — he knew variation existed and was inherited, but had no idea how. He proposed "pangenesis" — that cells shed particles called gemmules that migrate to the gonads — a hypothesis that was completely wrong and was disproved in his lifetime by Galton's blood transfusion experiments.
⚠ He worried deeply about blending inheritance — the reasonable Victorian assumption that offspring traits are the average of their parents', which would erode variation and make natural selection impossible. Mendel's particulate genetics (published 1866, rediscovered 1900) solved this problem, but Darwin never read Mendel and died unaware of the solution.
⚠ His theory of sexual selection was largely ignored or mocked for a century — the idea that female choice could drive male evolution seemed to many scientists anthropomorphic and implausible. It was vindicated only by the mathematical work of Fisher (1930) and the experimental work of behavioural ecologists from the 1970s onward.
⚠ Darwin underestimated the role of genetic drift — random changes in allele frequency in small populations — as an evolutionary force independent of selection. This was formalised by Sewall Wright in the 1930s and shown to be crucial in small, isolated populations exactly like those Darwin studied on the Galápagos.
⚠ His view of evolution was gradualist — natura non facit saltus, "nature makes no leaps" — and he struggled with the fossil record's apparent gaps and the sudden appearance of major body plans. Punctuated equilibrium (Gould & Eldredge, 1972) later showed that evolution can proceed in rapid bursts separated by long stasis, partly reconciling the fossil record with Darwinian theory.
