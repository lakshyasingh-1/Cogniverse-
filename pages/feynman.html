01   Biography

The Curious
Character

Richard Phillips Feynman was born in Queens, New York in 1918. Raised by a father who taught him to see patterns instead of labels, he arrived at MIT and then Princeton already thinking differently from everyone around him.

At twenty-four he joined the Manhattan Project at Los Alamos, where his combination of brilliance and irreverence — safecracking, bongo drums, letters written in code to evade censors — became the stuff of legend. After the war, at Cornell and then Caltech, he single-handedly reformulated quantum mechanics through a new tool he invented: the path integral, and gave QED its now-iconic diagrammatic language.

He shared the 1965 Nobel Prize in Physics with Schwinger and Tomonaga for this work. But Feynman was equally legendary as a teacher — his undergraduate lectures at Caltech, published as The Feynman Lectures on Physics, remain the greatest physics textbook ever written. In his final act he exposed the O-ring failure that destroyed Challenger, demonstrating the fault with a glass of ice water on live television.

Path integral: the probability amplitude for a particle to travel between two points is a sum over every possible path, each weighted by its classical action.

QED Lagrangian: governs how electrons and photons interact. Feynman's diagrammatic rules for this theory predict experimental results to 12 decimal places.

Fine-structure constant: the dimensionless coupling strength of electromagnetism. Feynman called it "one of the greatest damn mysteries of physics."
ΔE · Δt ≥ ℏ/2
Energy-time uncertainty: a virtual particle can violate energy conservation briefly. Feynman diagrams made these fleeting interactions calculable and physical.
P = |∑ amplitudes|²
The core rule of quantum mechanics as Feynman taught it: compute an amplitude (a complex number) for each way an event can happen, add them all up, then square the result to get probability. Simple to state; revolutionary in application.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
Feynman diagrams — a pictorial calculus for particle interactions that transformed abstract QED into a tool every physicist could use.
02
Path integral formulation — a completely new and mathematically equivalent foundation for quantum mechanics, now used across all of theoretical physics.
03
Parton model — proposed that protons contain point-like constituents (quarks), paving the way for quantum chromodynamics (QCD).
04
Quantum computing — his 1981 lecture "Simulating Physics with Computers" laid the conceptual foundation for the entire field.
05
The Feynman Lectures on Physics — three volumes of undergraduate physics reimagined from first principles, still the most read physics text in the world.
∞
…and an entire generation of physicists who learned to love the mystery of it all.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Feynman Stumbled

⚠ QED cannot be a complete theory of nature — it doesn't include gravity, and breaks down at the Planck scale.
⚠ Expressed deep scepticism of string theory and quantum gravity research, dismissing entire research programmes that later bore fruit.
⚠ His renormalisation procedure, while effective, was regarded by Dirac and others as mathematically suspect — the underlying issue remains unresolved.
⚠ Never produced a grand unified theory or made substantial contributions to general relativity or cosmology.
⚠ His famous "if you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics" — a frank admission that the interpretation of the theory remains contested to this day.
