01   Biography

The Man Who
Bent the Circle

Johannes Kepler was born on 27 December 1571 in Weil der Stadt, in the Holy Roman Empire, into a family of precarious circumstances — his father a mercenary soldier who eventually vanished, his mother an herbalist who would later be tried for witchcraft. He was a sickly child whose smallpox left him with damaged eyesight, a bitter irony for the man who would become one of history's greatest observational theorists. It was a childhood vision of the Great Conjunction of Saturn and Jupiter in 1577, and a lunar eclipse in 1580, that turned him toward the sky.

He studied at the University of Tübingen under Michael Maestlin, one of the few academics who privately accepted the Copernican heliocentric model. Kepler became a convinced Copernican. Appointed mathematics teacher in Graz in 1594, he published his first major work, Mysterium Cosmographicum (1596), proposing that the spacings of the planetary orbits corresponded to the five Platonic solids nested inside each other. The model was wrong, but it announced a mind of unusual ambition and geometric imagination, and it caught the attention of Tycho Brahe.

In 1600 Kepler joined Brahe in Prague as his assistant, inheriting upon Brahe's death in 1601 the most precise observational archive in astronomical history. He spent the next eight years wrestling above all with Mars — the planet whose orbit deviated most stubbornly from any circular model. After nearly a thousand pages of calculation, discarding a model that was off by only eight arc-minutes because he trusted Brahe's data more than any theory, he arrived at the ellipse. Astronomia Nova (1609) announced the first two laws of planetary motion. A decade later, Harmonices Mundi (1619) contained the third. Newton would later use all three as the empirical foundation of his law of universal gravitation — the keystone of classical physics.
  Major Achievements
  • Discovered the three laws of planetary motion (1609–1619)
  • Proved planetary orbits are ellipses, not circles
  • Published Astronomia Nova — a founding document of modern astronomy
  • Compiled the Rudolphine Tables — most accurate planetary tables of the era
  • Founded modern optics in Astronomiae Pars Optica (1604)

02   Laws & Principles

The Three Laws
That Govern Orbits

The Three Laws of Planetary Motion
Law I
The Ellipse Law
Planets orbit the Sun in ellipses, with the Sun at one focus — 1609
Law II
The Equal Areas Law
A planet sweeps equal areas in equal times — 1609
Law III
The Harmonic Law
T² ∝ a³ — the orbital period squared scales with semi-axis cubed — 1619
r = a(1 − e²) / (1 + e·cosθ)
First Law in polar form: a planet's orbit is a conic section (ellipse) with the Sun at one focus. The parameter e is the eccentricity — zero for a circle, between 0 and 1 for an ellipse. Earth's eccentricity is 0.0167; Mars's is 0.093, which is precisely why Mars revealed the truth.
dA/dt = L / 2m = constant
Second Law: the rate at which a planet sweeps area is constant — equivalent, in modern terms, to conservation of angular momentum. A planet moves fastest at perihelion (closest to the Sun) and slowest at aphelion (farthest). Newton later showed this follows from any central force.
T² / a³ = 4π² / GM
Third Law, modern form: the square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its semi-major axis. Kepler found this empirically in 1619; Newton derived it theoretically from his law of gravitation in 1687 — the single most elegant confirmation that Kepler's laws were not coincidences.
n = (1/f) sin(r) / sin(i)
Kepler's approximation of the law of refraction (Snell's Law) — the first quantitative attempt to describe how light bends at an interface. Though not exact, it was a landmark step in founding theoretical optics, published in Dioptrice (1611).
F = GMm/r²  →  derived from  T² ∝ a³  +  elliptical orbit  [Newton, 1687]
The ultimate consequence: Newton demonstrated that his inverse-square law of gravitation mathematically implies all three of Kepler's laws. This was the moment science acquired a unified mechanical explanation for the motion of every body in the solar system. Kepler never knew why his laws worked — he searched for musical harmonies and geometric mysticism. Newton revealed the answer was gravity. Kepler laid the rail; Newton laid the engine on it.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
The three laws of planetary motion — the empirical foundation of orbital mechanics, used to this day to calculate the trajectories of every spacecraft, satellite, and probe humanity has ever launched.
02
The death of the perfect circle — by proving orbits are ellipses, he ended two thousand years of insistence, from Plato through Copernicus, that celestial motion must be circular. He freed astronomy from its most ancient constraint.
03
The Rudolphine Tables (1627) — planetary position tables based on Brahe's observations and his own laws, accurate to within one arc-minute, far surpassing all predecessors and used by navigators and astronomers for a century.
04
The foundation of modern optics — his Astronomiae Pars Optica (1604) described atmospheric refraction, the camera obscura, and how the eye forms images on the retina. Dioptrice (1611) analysed the telescope for the first time mathematically.
05
The empirical path to Newton — his three laws were the precise quantitative constraints that allowed Newton to derive universal gravitation. Without Kepler's numbers, Newton's theory could not have been tested or even formulated.
∞
…and the proof that the universe obeys mathematical laws — a conviction he held on almost mystical grounds, but which turned out to be literally, precisely, measurably true.

P.01
Solved the orbit of Mars — the central computational problem of his era. Every circular model of Mars disagreed with Brahe's observations by eight arc-minutes. Kepler alone treated this discrepancy as a refutation rather than an error, and eight years of calculation resolved it with the ellipse.

Explained the optical mechanism of vision — he was the first to correctly describe that the retina, not the lens, is the seat of the image, and that the lens inverts the image which the brain then corrects. 05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Kepler Stumbled

⚠ Kepler had no explanation for why the planets moved as they did. He speculated about a "moving spirit" or anima motrix emanating from the Sun — a quasi-mystical force. The actual explanation, gravity, lay sixty years in the future with Newton.
⚠ His first major work, Mysterium Cosmographicum, was entirely wrong — proposing that the five Platonic solids determined the spacing of planetary orbits. He remained emotionally attached to this geometric mysticism throughout his life, even after his own ellipse law made it untenable.
⚠ He was a practising astrologer who cast horoscopes to supplement his income — including for Albrecht von Wallenstein, the imperial general. He made a sharp distinction between astronomy and astrology in principle but observed none in practice.
⚠ Kepler struggled his entire life with religious and professional conflict — expelled from Graz for being Lutheran, unable to fully reconcile his science with his theology, and embroiled for years in defending his mother in her witchcraft trial. These disruptions cost him years of productive work.
⚠ He never embraced Galileo's telescopic discoveries as fully as posterity might expect. Though he corresponded with Galileo and published support for the telescope, he was slow to integrate Galileo's mechanics with his own orbital work — a synthesis that waited for Newton.
