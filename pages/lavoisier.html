01   Biography

A Life That
Transformed Matter

Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier was born in Paris into a wealthy bourgeois family. He studied law at his father's insistence, but his true passion was science. Elected to the Académie Royale des Sciences at age 25 — unusually young — he threw himself into a systematic reform of chemistry that would, within two decades, make the entire discipline unrecognisable.

Working in a private laboratory of extraordinary precision — funded partly by his role as a tax-farmer for the Crown, a role that would later doom him — Lavoisier dismantled the centuries-old phlogiston theory of combustion. Using careful quantitative measurement with precision balances, he showed that combustion was not the release of a mysterious substance called phlogiston, but the combination of matter with oxygen — a gas he named and characterised.

His masterwork, Traité Élémentaire de Chimie (1789), presented the first modern list of chemical elements, introduced the law of conservation of mass, and gave chemistry its modern nomenclature. It is often called the first modern chemistry textbook. Lavoisier also discovered that respiration was a form of slow combustion — the first step toward biochemistry. In 1794, at the height of the Terror, he was convicted on false charges by the Revolutionary Tribunal and guillotined. The mathematician Lagrange mourned: "It took them only a moment to cut off that head, and a hundred years may not produce another like it."
  Major Achievements
  • Proved the Law of Conservation of Mass — matter is neither created nor destroyed
  • Identified and named oxygen and hydrogen
  • Demolished the phlogiston theory of combustion
  • Showed that combustion and respiration are both oxidation processes
  • Introduced systematic chemical nomenclature (with Berthollet, Fourcroy, Guyton)
  • Published Traité Élémentaire de Chimie — first modern chemistry textbook (1789)
  • Introduced the concept of the chemical element as an operationally defined substance
  • First calorimetric measurements of metabolic heat in animals
02   Laws & Equations

The Mathematics
of Matter

mreactants = mproducts
Law of Conservation of Mass — Lavoisier's supreme discovery: in any chemical reaction, the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. Matter is rearranged, never destroyed or created.
C + O₂ → CO₂
Combustion as oxidation — Lavoisier's decisive reframing: burning is combination with oxygen, not release of phlogiston. This single conceptual revolution made modern chemistry possible.
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + E
Aerobic respiration — the biochemical process Lavoisier first identified as analogous to combustion. He showed that animals consume oxygen and produce CO₂, generating heat — founding the science of metabolism.
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Synthesis of water — Lavoisier proved that water is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen (not an element), exploding the ancient four-element theory and giving hydrogen its name from the Greek for "water-maker."
Q = m · c · ΔT   (calorimetry)
Calorimetric heat measurement — Lavoisier and Laplace built the first ice calorimeter in 1782, measuring the heat produced by chemical reactions and animal respiration with precision. This equation captures the principle: heat equals mass times specific heat times temperature change, founding thermochemistry and the quantitative study of metabolism.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Chemistry

01
Law of Conservation of Mass — the bedrock of all chemistry and physics: in a closed system, the total mass of matter is constant, no matter what chemical reactions occur.
02
Discovery and naming of oxygen — isolated, characterised, and named the gas that drives combustion and respiration, replacing phlogiston with a chemically real substance.
03
Modern chemical nomenclature — working with three colleagues, Lavoisier systematised chemical naming so that a compound's name reflects its composition. The system survives essentially unchanged to this day.
04
First modern chemistry textbook — Traité Élémentaire de Chimie (1789) presented the new oxygen-based chemistry, listed 33 elements, and defined the chemical element operationally as any substance that cannot be further decomposed.
05
Discovery of the role of oxygen in respiration and metabolism — Lavoisier showed that breathing is slow combustion, measuring the oxygen consumed and CO₂ produced by a guinea pig — the birth of biochemistry.
⚗
…and the transformation of chemistry from an art of recipes and mystical substances into a rigorous, quantitative, predictive science — the Chemical Revolution.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Lavoisier Stumbled

⚠ He mistakenly believed that all acids must contain oxygen — coining the name "oxygen" from the Greek for "acid-maker" (ὀξύς + γεννάω). Davy disproved this in 1810 by showing hydrochloric acid (HCl) contains no oxygen whatsoever.
⚠ He listed "light" and "caloric" (heat) as chemical elements in his Traité — physical imponderable fluids that he could not yet distinguish from matter. The caloric theory of heat was not disproven until Rumford's experiments of 1798, four years after Lavoisier's death.
⚠ Lavoisier's role as a Fermier Général — a private tax collector for the French Crown — made him deeply unpopular with the poor and politically vulnerable. He failed to read the political dangers of the Revolution and was guillotined in 1794 on charges that were largely fabricated but rooted in his tax-farming activities.
⚠ He systematically underacknowledged the contributions of Joseph Priestley, who had independently isolated oxygen before him, and of Carl Wilhelm Scheele, who had actually discovered it first. Lavoisier's superior experimental framework won history's verdict, but at the cost of serious scientific credit to others.
⚠ Despite his meticulous quantitative approach, he could not explain why elements combine in fixed ratios — that had to wait for Dalton's atomic theory (1803). Lavoisier's chemistry was macroscopic and phenomenological; the atomic model that gave it its full explanatory power came a decade after his death.
