01   Biography

A Life That
Lit Up the World

James Clerk Maxwell was born in Edinburgh in 1831 into a Scottish landowning family with a keen interest in science. A precocious child, he published his first mathematical paper — on the geometry of oval curves — at age 14. He studied at the University of Edinburgh and then at Cambridge, where he graduated as Second Wrangler and won the Adams Prize.

His supreme achievement was the formulation of the four equations bearing his name, published in A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism (1873). They unified electricity, magnetism, and optics into a single coherent electromagnetic theory and predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves travelling at the speed of light — one of the greatest feats of theoretical physics in history.

Einstein kept a portrait of Maxwell on his study wall alongside Newton and Faraday. He called Maxwell's work "the most profound and the most fruitful that physics has experienced since the time of Newton." Maxwell died of abdominal cancer at just 48, leaving physics forever transformed.
  Major Achievements
  • Formulated Maxwell's Equations — the foundation of classical electromagnetism
  • Predicted electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light
  • Developed the kinetic theory of gases (Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution)
  • First permanent colour photograph (1861)
  • Maxwell's Demon — a landmark thought experiment in thermodynamics
  • Saturn's rings shown to be particulate (Adams Prize, 1857)
  • Founded the Cavendish Laboratory at Cambridge
02   Equations

Four Lines That
Explained Everything

∇ · E = ρ/ε₀
Gauss's Law: electric charges create electric fields that radiate outward. The total flux through a closed surface equals the enclosed charge over permittivity.
∇ · B = 0
Gauss's Law for Magnetism: magnetic monopoles do not exist. Magnetic field lines form closed loops — they have no beginning or end.
∇ × E = −∂B/∂t
Faraday's Law: a changing magnetic field induces a curling electric field. The basis of every electric generator and transformer ever built.
∇ × B = μ₀(J + ε₀ ∂E/∂t)
Ampère–Maxwell Law: currents and changing electric fields create magnetic fields. Maxwell's displacement current term predicted electromagnetic waves.
c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s
Speed of light from first principles: derived purely from electromagnetic constants, Maxwell showed light itself is an electromagnetic wave — one of the most stunning deductions in science.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
Maxwell's Equations — four compact laws that unified all known electrical and magnetic phenomena and predicted radio waves, X-rays, and visible light as a spectrum.
02
Kinetic theory of gases — the Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution explained temperature, pressure, and viscosity from the statistical motion of molecules.
03
First durable colour photograph (1861) — demonstrated the three-colour process by photographing a tartan ribbon, founding colour imaging theory.
04
Maxwell's Demon — a thought experiment that probed the relationship between information, entropy, and the Second Law of Thermodynamics, still debated today.
05
Proved Saturn's rings must consist of countless small particles — not solid or liquid — 140 years before the Voyager probes confirmed it directly.
∿
…and the theoretical seed from which Hertz discovered radio waves, Marconi built the telegraph, and Einstein forged special relativity.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Maxwell Stumbled

⚠ His equations required the existence of a luminiferous aether as the medium for electromagnetic waves — a concept the Michelson-Morley experiment (1887) demolished, paving the way for Einstein's relativity.
⚠ He never achieved a fully mechanical model of the electromagnetic field — his "idle wheel" vortex model was a scaffolding he himself knew was incomplete, not a final theory.
⚠ Maxwell's Demon appeared to violate the Second Law of Thermodynamics. He had no resolution — it took Szilárd (1929) and Landauer (1961) to show that information erasure, not sorting, costs entropy.
⚠ His kinetic theory assumed perfectly elastic collisions and classical statistics — it could not account for the quantum behaviour of gases at low temperatures, later explained by Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics.
⚠ He died at 48 before he could respond to Hertz's 1887 experimental confirmation of electromagnetic waves — he never saw the full triumphant vindication of his greatest prediction.
