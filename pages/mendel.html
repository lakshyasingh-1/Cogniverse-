01   Biography

The Friar Who
Cracked Heredity

Johann Mendel was born on 20 July 1822 in Hynčice, a small village in Moravia (now the Czech Republic), the son of peasant farmers who scraped through hard harvests and hard winters. Intellectually exceptional from childhood, he was sent by his schoolmaster to the Gymnasium in Opava at eleven — and supported himself through university by tutoring, driven to the edge of nervous collapse by financial stress. At twenty-one he entered the Augustinian monastery of St. Thomas in Brno, taking the name Gregor, finding in the Abbey not just spiritual sustenance but a community of serious natural scientists and access to its remarkable library.

Sent by the Abbey to the University of Vienna from 1851 to 1853, he studied physics under Christian Doppler and botany under Franz Unger — acquiring both the statistical rigour and the botanical grounding that would define his great experiment. Returning to Brno, he began in 1856 what would become eight years of meticulous experimental work in the Abbey's kitchen garden: crossing and back-crossing varieties of Pisum sativum (garden pea) across seven discrete traits, tracking ratios across generations with obsessive precision, growing 29,000 plants in total.

His two papers — presented to the Natural History Society of Brno in 1865 and published in 1866 — were a work of extraordinary originality. He proposed that heritable traits are controlled by discrete particles (he called them Faktoren, later named genes), that each organism carries two copies of each factor, and that the two copies segregate independently into reproductive cells. The ratios he observed — 3:1 in the second filial generation, 1:2:1 in genotype — followed directly from these hypotheses with mathematical precision.

The papers were ignored. Only forty reprints were requested from the journal. Darwin, who was working on pangenesis at exactly the same moment, never read them. Mendel was elected Abbot of St. Thomas in 1868 and spent his final years consumed by administrative duties and a losing battle with the Austrian government over monastery taxation. He died in 1884, unrecognised. In 1900, three botanists — de Vries, Correns, and Tschermak — independently rediscovered Mendelian ratios and, searching the literature, found Mendel's 1866 paper. The science of genetics was born, thirty-five years after its founding paper.

03   Laws & Equations

The Mathematics
of Heredity

P(dominant) = 3/4  ·  P(recessive) = 1/4
The 3:1 phenotypic ratio in the F₂ generation — Mendel's first great quantitative result. Crossing two F₁ hybrids (Rr × Rr) yields RR, Rr, Rr, rr in equal proportions. Since R is dominant, three-quarters of plants display the dominant trait and one quarter the recessive. Observed in all seven traits across 7,324 plants.
Genotype ratio: 1 RR : 2 Rr : 1 rr
The 1:2:1 genotypic ratio underlying the 3:1 phenotypic ratio. Mendel verified this by back-crossing F₂ plants and observing which bred true (RR) and which segregated again (Rr). This inner ratio proved that heterozygotes (Rr) are distinct from homozygous dominants (RR) — a conceptual revolution in the understanding of heredity.
P(AaBb) = P(Aa) × P(Bb)
Law of Independent Assortment: the probability of inheriting a particular combination of alleles at two independent loci equals the product of the individual probabilities. Dihybrid crosses yield a 9:3:3:1 ratio in the F₂ generation. This holds when the two genes are on different chromosomes — the mechanistic explanation Mendel did not yet have.
AaBb × AaBb → 9:3:3:1
Dihybrid cross result: when two independent traits are crossed simultaneously, the F₂ offspring appear in ratio 9 (A_B_) : 3 (A_bb) : 3 (aaB_) : 1 (aabb). Mendel verified this ratio across combinations of seed shape and colour, confirming independent assortment across 556 seeds.
Hardy-Weinberg: p² + 2pq + q² = 1   (p + q = 1)
The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (1908) — the direct mathematical extension of Mendel's laws to population genetics. In a large, randomly mating population with no selection, mutation, or drift, allele frequencies p and q remain constant across generations. This is the null model of population genetics: Mendelian inheritance with no evolutionary change. It quantified what Mendel showed qualitatively: that heritable variation is preserved, not blended away, across generations — the answer to Darwin's greatest unsolved problem.
04   Contributions

What He
Gave Biology

01
The gene concept — proposed that heritable traits are controlled by discrete, particulate factors (later named genes) that retain their identity across generations rather than blending into an average.
02
Law of Segregation — proved that the two copies of each heritable factor separate during reproduction so that each gamete carries exactly one copy, restoring the pair at fertilisation.
03
Law of Independent Assortment — showed that different traits are inherited independently of one another (subject to the linkage exception discovered later by Morgan), producing all combinations of parental traits in predictable ratios.
04
Dominance and recessivity — introduced the conceptual vocabulary and experimental demonstration that some alleles mask others, explaining why traits can disappear in one generation and reappear unchanged in the next.
05
Statistical biology — first scientist to apply quantitative, statistical methods to biological inheritance, transforming biology from a descriptive into a predictive science and anticipating by decades the mathematical biology of Fisher and Wright.
∞
…and the answer to Darwin's most urgent unsolved problem — why variation is not erased by inheritance.
05   Problems Solved

The Unsolvable,
Solved

P.01
Explained how discrete heritable traits are transmitted from parent to offspring with predictable mathematical ratios — replacing speculative blending theories with a rigorous particulate model verified across seven independent traits and tens of thousands of plants.
Source: Genetics Proof: Versuche über Pflanzenhybriden (1866)
P.02
Solved Darwin's "swamping problem" — Darwin could not explain why natural selection would not be undone by blending inheritance, which would halve any new variation in every generation. Mendel's particulate factors showed that variation is preserved intact indefinitely.
Source: Evolutionary Biology Proof: Hardy-Weinberg principle (1908), built on Mendel
P.03
Explained the reappearance of recessive traits after skipping generations — a universal puzzle in pre-Mendelian biology. His heterozygote (Rr) concept showed that a trait can be carried invisibly and re-emerge unchanged when two carriers mate.
Source: Inheritance Patterns Proof: F₂ back-crossing experiments (1860–1863)
P.04
Proved that different heritable traits are transmitted independently of one another — contradicting the prevailing assumption that all features of a parent were inherited as a bundle, and enabling the prediction of all possible offspring combinations from dihybrid and polyhybrid crosses.
Source: Multi-trait Inheritance Proof: Dihybrid crosses, 9:3:3:1 ratio (1864–1865)
P.05
Established that biology can be a quantitative, predictive science — demonstrating that living systems obey mathematical laws as precise as those of physics, a paradigm shift that founded quantitative genetics, molecular biology, and genomics.
Source: Philosophy of Biology Proof: Statistical analysis of 40,000+ trait observations
06   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Mendel Stumbled

⚠ Mendel's data are suspiciously close to theoretical predictions — far closer than chance would normally produce over 29,000 plants. Ronald Fisher's 1936 statistical analysis concluded the data had likely been unconsciously or consciously adjusted. The precise extent of any manipulation remains one of biology's most debated historical controversies.
⚠ His Law of Independent Assortment is not universal — genes on the same chromosome are linked and inherited together, as Thomas Hunt Morgan proved in 1911. Mendel happened to choose seven traits that happen to lie on different chromosomes (or far enough apart to recombine freely), an extraordinarily lucky experimental choice.
⚠ Mendel had no mechanistic explanation for his factors — he knew they existed and obeyed mathematical laws but had no account of what they were physically, where they were located, or how they segregated. The chromosomal theory of inheritance, connecting Mendel's factors to observable cellular structures, was not established until Sutton and Boveri in 1902–1903.
⚠ His later experiments on hawkweed (Hieracium), suggested to him by Carl Nägeli, failed to reproduce Mendelian ratios — because hawkweed reproduces mainly apomictically (asexually). This failure shook Mendel's own confidence in his pea results and may have contributed to his declining engagement with biology after 1868.
⚠ His work was ignored for 34 years — a failure not of the science but of scientific communication. He published in a regional journal, presented to a provincial society, and corresponded with Nägeli who never grasped the significance of what he was reading. The history of science has rarely seen a more catastrophic mismatch between a discovery and its reception.
