01   Biography

The Man Who
Invented the Bond

Linus Carl Pauling was born on 28 February 1901 in Portland, Oregon. Largely self-educated as a teenager after his father's early death, he devoured chemistry books and performed kitchen experiments before entering Oregon State College at sixteen. By the time he arrived at Caltech for his doctorate in 1922, he had already begun thinking about the question that would define his career: what holds atoms together?

Armed with the newly developed tools of X-ray crystallography and the quantum mechanics arriving from Europe, Pauling spent the 1930s working out the rules of the chemical bond from first principles. His 1939 masterwork, The Nature of the Chemical Bond, synthesised quantum mechanics, crystallography, and chemical intuition into a unified framework — introducing electronegativity, resonance, orbital hybridisation, and the concept of partial ionic character. It is the most cited scientific book of the 20th century. He received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1954.

In the 1950s, racing Watson and Crick, he proposed the alpha-helix and beta-sheet structures of proteins — the two most important structural motifs in biochemistry. He narrowly missed the structure of DNA (his triple-helix model was wrong) but his methods shaped the entire field of structural biology. Simultaneously, he became a fierce anti-nuclear campaigner, gathering signatures from over 11,000 scientists for a petition to halt weapons testing. This earned him the Nobel Peace Prize in 1962 — announced, with perfect irony, on the day the Limited Nuclear Test Ban Treaty was signed.

02   Equations

The Mathematics
of Chemical Bonding

χA − χB = 0.102√(ΔD)
Pauling electronegativity: the difference in electronegativity between two atoms equals 0.102 times the square root of the extra bond energy. χ determines whether bonds are ionic, covalent, or polar — the single most useful number in chemistry.
ψbond = c₁ψcov + c₂ψion
Resonance hybrid wavefunction: a chemical bond is not purely covalent or ionic but a quantum superposition of both. The coefficients c₁ and c₂ determine its character — the foundation of all modern bonding theory.
sp³ : ψ = s ± pₓ ± p_y ± p_z
Orbital hybridisation: combining s and p atomic orbitals produces new hybrid orbitals (sp, sp², sp³) pointing at specific angles. This explains tetrahedral carbon, planar benzene, and the geometry of every organic molecule.
rAB = rA + rB − 0.09|χA−χB|
Pauling bond-length rule: the length of a polar bond is shorter than the sum of covalent radii by an amount proportional to the electronegativity difference. Used to predict bond lengths across the periodic table.
n − s = 2  (Pauling's rules)   ·   φ = 100° (α-helix rise per residue ≈ 1.5 Å, 3.6 residues/turn)
Pauling's rules for ionic crystals & alpha-helix parameters: his five rules for predicting stable crystal structures from ionic radii, and his precise geometric description of the alpha-helix — the dominant secondary structure of proteins. Both were derived from pure geometric reasoning about bond angles, lengths, and hydrogen bonding constraints before modern computing existed.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Science

01
The Nature of the Chemical Bond — a unified quantum-mechanical framework for understanding why atoms bond, how bond lengths and angles arise, and what determines molecular structure.
02
Electronegativity — a quantitative scale for every element's electron-attracting power, now the most-used single number in chemical education and research worldwide.
03
Protein secondary structure — discovery of the alpha-helix and beta-sheet from pure geometric reasoning about hydrogen bonds, founding structural biology as a discipline.
04
Molecular basis of disease — his 1949 paper on sickle-cell anaemia was the first demonstration that a disease could be traced to a single molecular defect, launching molecular medicine.
05
Nuclear disarmament — collected 11,000+ scientist signatures on the Mainau Declaration against nuclear weapons, contributing directly to the 1963 Limited Nuclear Test Ban Treaty.
∞
…and the only scientific career in history to win two unshared Nobel Prizes in entirely different fields.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Pauling Stumbled

⚠ He proposed a triple-helix model for DNA in 1953 — a confident, widely circulated error. Watson and Crick beat him to the correct double-helix structure partly because he lacked access to Rosalind Franklin's superior X-ray data.
⚠ His later advocacy for high-dose Vitamin C as a cure for cancer and the common cold was not supported by rigorous clinical evidence — he became increasingly isolated from mainstream medicine and pursued this claim to the end of his life.
⚠ His resonance theory, while phenomenologically useful, was criticised as having no rigorous quantum mechanical foundation — it is a language for describing molecules, not a derivable theory.
⚠ He was slow to accept the existence of quasicrystals — when Dan Shechtman announced their discovery in 1982, Pauling publicly dismissed the claim repeatedly. Shechtman won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2011.
⚠ His FBI surveillance file ran to hundreds of pages; the State Department twice denied him a passport, hampering his international collaborations at the height of his scientific productivity during the McCarthy era.
