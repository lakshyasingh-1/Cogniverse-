01   Biography

The Man Who
Knew Infinity

Srinivasa Ramanujan Iyengar was born on 22 December 1887 in Erode, in the Madras Presidency of British India. Raised in a Brahmin family of modest means in Kumbakonam, he showed an almost supernatural facility with numbers from childhood — by age twelve he had mastered a college-level trigonometry textbook; by sixteen, he had independently rediscovered Euler's results on continued fractions and divergent series.

So consumed was he by mathematics that he failed his university examinations twice, losing his scholarships and spending years in poverty, filling notebooks with results he could not publish or communicate. In 1913, in an act of extraordinary audacity, he wrote a letter to G. H. Hardy at Cambridge, filled with a hundred theorems and no proofs. Hardy, after sharing it with Littlewood, wrote back that Ramanujan must be a mathematician of the highest class. He arranged passage to England.

The five years at Cambridge (1914–1919) were among the most productive in mathematical history. Hardy and Ramanujan collaborated on the revolutionary circle method for number partitions. Ramanujan became the first Indian elected Fellow of the Royal Society, and the first Indian Fellow of Trinity College. He returned to India in 1919 gravely ill with tuberculosis, and died at 32 — leaving behind three notebooks and a mysterious "lost notebook" rediscovered in 1976, whose contents mathematicians are still proving today.

02   Equations

Identities from
Another World

1/π = (2√2/9801) ∑ (4n)!(1103+26390n) / (n!)⁴ 396⁴ⁿ
Ramanujan's series for 1/π: each term adds roughly 8 correct digits of π. It was the basis for the fastest algorithms to compute π for decades — including the first billion-digit calculations.
p(n) ~ eπ√(2n/3) / (4n√3)
Hardy–Ramanujan partition formula: an asymptotic count of how many ways a number n can be written as a sum of positive integers. It founded the circle method — the most powerful tool in analytic combinatorics.
1 + 2 + 3 + ··· = −1/12
Ramanujan summation of divergent series: in the sense of zeta regularisation, ζ(−1) = −1/12. This result appears in bosonic string theory and the Casimir effect — nature uses Ramanujan's sum.
∑n=0∞ qn² = ∏n=1∞ (1−q5n)(1−q5n−1)−1…
Rogers–Ramanujan identity: a breathtaking equality between a sum over squares and an infinite product. Rediscovered independently; its combinatorial meaning took decades to unlock, and it still appears in statistical mechanics.
τ(n) = n11 ∑d|n μ(d) · d−11 · σ11(n/d)  ·  Δ(z) = q ∏n=1∞(1−qⁿ)24
Ramanujan's tau function & the discriminant modular form Δ(z): Ramanujan conjectured remarkable properties of τ(n) in 1916 — including that |τ(p)| ≤ 2p11/2 for primes p. This conjecture, proved by Deligne in 1974 using the full machinery of algebraic geometry and earning him the Fields Medal, is one of the deepest results in 20th-century mathematics.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave Mathematics

01
The circle method (with Hardy) — a technique for extracting asymptotic information from generating functions, now the backbone of analytic combinatorics and additive number theory.
02
Mock theta functions — mysterious objects introduced in his final letter to Hardy that defied classification for 80 years, until Zwegers resolved them in 2002 using the theory of harmonic Maass forms.
03
Highly composite numbers — he systematically studied numbers with unusually many divisors, founding a theory with applications from signal processing to algorithm design.
04
Infinite series for π — his formulas converge so rapidly they became the computational engine behind every record-breaking calculation of π digits in the modern era.
05
The lost notebook — rediscovered by George Andrews in 1976 in a box at Trinity College Library, it contained over 600 results still being proved today, spawning entire new subfields.
∞
…and three notebooks of unproved results that mathematicians will spend the next century verifying.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Ramanujan Stumbled

⚠ Ramanujan rarely provided proofs — his notebooks are full of results stated without justification. Some have been found to be wrong; others remain unverified a century later for want of a proof.
⚠ His early work on divergent series and the "sum" 1+2+3+⋯ = −1/12 was not rigorous by contemporary standards — it was intuitive and correct in the right sense, but required a century of formalism to properly justify.
⚠ He independently rediscovered many known results — a consequence of his isolation from the mathematical literature. Much of his early energy was spent on mathematics that Europe had already settled.
⚠ His deep intuition did not extend uniformly across mathematics — his work was almost entirely in analysis, number theory, and combinatorics; geometry, algebra, and topology left few traces in his notebooks.
⚠ The loss of his life at 32 is itself the greatest limitation — Hardy later said that Ramanujan's death was the one great tragedy of his own mathematical life, and that what was lost can never be estimated.
