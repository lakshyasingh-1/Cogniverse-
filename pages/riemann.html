01   Biography

A Short Life of
Infinite Depth

Georg Friedrich Bernhard Riemann was born in 1826 in Breselenz, a small village in the Kingdom of Hanover, the second of six children of a Lutheran pastor. Shy, sickly, and intensely devout, he was a self-taught mathematical prodigy who initially enrolled at Göttingen to study theology before Gauss recognised his gifts and redirected him entirely.

His 1851 doctoral dissertation on complex functions — supervised by the aged Gauss, who called it evidence of a gloriously fertile mind — introduced the concept of Riemann surfaces and transformed complex analysis. His 1854 habilitation lecture, "On the Hypotheses Which Lie at the Foundations of Geometry," delivered to a stunned audience that included Gauss himself, founded Riemannian geometry: a framework of curved spaces of arbitrary dimension that would, sixty years later, become the mathematical language of Einstein's general relativity.

In 1859 he published a single eight-page paper on prime numbers that introduced the Riemann zeta function, the Riemann hypothesis, and methods that became the foundation of all modern analytic number theory. He died of tuberculosis in 1866 at the age of 39, having published fewer than a dozen papers — each one opening a new branch of mathematics.

03   Contributions

What He
Gave Mathematics

01
Riemannian geometry — a complete framework for curved spaces of any dimension, the mathematical language Einstein needed for general relativity, invented 60 years before Einstein needed it.
02
The Riemann hypothesis — his 1859 conjecture about the zeros of ζ(s) remains the most important unsolved problem in mathematics, carrying a $1 million Millennium Prize.
03
Riemann surfaces — gave multi-valued complex functions a rigorous geometric home, founding modern topology and the study of algebraic curves.
04
The Riemann integral — the first fully rigorous definition of the definite integral, replacing centuries of geometric intuition with a precise mathematical foundation.
05
Analytic number theory — his single 1859 paper on primes introduced methods — the explicit formula, the prime-counting function — that defined the field for the next century.
∞
…and an unsolved problem that has consumed the greatest mathematical minds for over a century and a half.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Riemann Stumbled

⚠ His proof of the Riemann mapping theorem relied on Dirichlet's principle — an assumption that Weierstrass later showed was not always valid. The theorem is true, but Riemann's proof had a gap.
⚠ The Riemann hypothesis itself remains unproved — his most famous conjecture, posed almost as an aside in 1859, is the central open problem of mathematics 166 years later.
⚠ His geometric framework, while revolutionary, initially lacked the tensor calculus machinery needed to make it fully operational — that required Christoffel, Ricci, and Levi-Civita over the following decades.
⚠ Riemann's integral, while rigorous, proved insufficient for the needs of 20th-century analysis — Lebesgue's more powerful measure-theoretic integral superseded it in most advanced applications.
⚠ His tragically short life — dead at 39 of tuberculosis — meant dozens of ideas recorded only in unpublished notes. His housekeeper burned many papers after his death, an irreplaceable loss to mathematics.
