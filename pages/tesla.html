01   Biography

A Life That
Shocked the World

Nikola Tesla was born at midnight during a lightning storm in Smiljan, Serbia (now Croatia) — an omen, locals said, for the man who would spend his life commanding electricity. After studying engineering in Graz and Prague, he moved to Budapest, then Paris, then New York in 1884, where he arrived with four cents in his pocket and a letter of introduction to Thomas Edison.

The famous split with Edison over alternating versus direct current — the War of Currents — defined both men. Tesla's AC system, backed by George Westinghouse, won decisively: it could transmit power over vast distances with minimal loss. The Niagara Falls power station of 1895, built on Tesla's AC patents, became the template for every electrical grid on Earth.

Tesla held over 300 patents across radio, rotating magnetic fields, the induction motor, fluorescent lighting, X-ray research, remote control, and wireless transmission of energy. Brilliant, eccentric, and ultimately penniless, he died alone in a New York hotel room in 1943 — yet the unit of magnetic flux density now bears his name, and the modern world runs on his inventions.

02   Principles & Equations

The Mathematics
of Alternating Power

V(t) = Vpeak · sin(2πft)
Alternating voltage: the sinusoidal waveform Tesla championed — voltage oscillates at frequency f, enabling efficient long-distance power transmission impossible with Edison's DC.
ns = 120f / p
Synchronous speed of Tesla's rotating magnetic field: revolutions per minute equal 120 times frequency divided by number of poles — the heartbeat of every AC induction motor ever built.
fr = 1 / (2π√LC)
Tesla Coil resonant frequency: when inductance L and capacitance C resonate together, the circuit achieves maximum energy transfer — producing the spectacular high-voltage discharges Tesla is famous for.
P = √3 · VL · IL · cos φ
Three-phase AC power: total power in Tesla's polyphase system. The √3 factor arises from the 120° phase offset between the three alternating currents — enabling smooth, constant power delivery.
Vs / Vp = Ns / Np
Transformer turns ratio: the reason AC won the War of Currents. Voltage can be stepped up for lossless long-distance transmission, then stepped back down at the destination — an operation impossible with DC at the time.
03   Contributions

What He
Gave the World

01
AC induction motor and polyphase power system — the technology that powers virtually every electric motor, factory, and household appliance on Earth to this day.
02
The Tesla Coil — a resonant transformer capable of producing extremely high voltages, still used in radio transmitters, plasma globes, and particle accelerator research.
03
Radio — Tesla demonstrated wireless transmission in 1893, before Marconi's patents. The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in 1943 that Tesla's patents predated Marconi's key claims.
04
First practical remote control — Tesla demonstrated a radio-controlled boat in Madison Square Garden in 1898, creating the conceptual blueprint for robotics and drone technology.
05
Fluorescent and neon lighting — Tesla demonstrated fluorescent tubes in 1893, decades before they became commercially available, anticipating the lighting technology of the 20th century.
⚡
…and a vision of wireless global energy so far ahead of its time that the world is only now beginning to catch up.

05   Limitations & Blind Spots

Where Even
Tesla Stumbled

⚠ Wardenclyffe Tower — his grand vision of wireless global power transmission — was never completed. Financier J.P. Morgan withdrew funding in 1906, and the project collapsed, leaving Tesla bankrupt and the concept unproven at scale.
⚠ His feud with Thomas Edison was partly self-destructive — Tesla's refusal to compromise or commercialise incrementally meant he repeatedly lost financial backing and credit to more business-savvy rivals.
⚠ He dismissed radio waves as a practical communication medium, believing they would never propagate beyond line of sight — a miscalculation that allowed Marconi to seize the commercial radio market while Tesla pursued wireless power.
⚠ His later years were marked by increasingly speculative and scientifically unfounded claims — a "death ray" (teleforce weapon), communication with Martians, and cosmic ray motors — damaging his credibility among physicists.
⚠ He kept few systematic records and worked largely from memory and intuition. Unlike Edison's meticulous notebooks, Tesla's method made it hard to defend patent priority, costing him credit for radio and other inventions.
